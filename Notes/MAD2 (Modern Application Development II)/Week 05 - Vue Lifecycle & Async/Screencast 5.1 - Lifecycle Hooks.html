The House, Stage by Stage

📐

Initialization

beforeCreatecreated

beforeCreate: the architect has just been hired, but no foundation exists yet — you can't ask "what's in the kitchen," because nothing has been built. Data and watchers aren't set up.

created: blueprints, plumbing, and wiring plans are finalized and the utilities are set up on paper — data, computed values, and methods all exist — but the house isn't standing at its address yet. A common time to pre-order furniture (fetch initial data).

🏗️

Mounting

beforeMountmounted

beforeMount: the prefab house sections are fully built off-site and are being craned onto the empty lot right now — the "for sale" sign is about to be swapped for an actual house.

mounted: the house is standing at its address, doors open, move-in day. The element genuinely exists now — the best time to arrange furniture (DOM manipulation) or schedule deliveries (backend data fetching).

🔨

Updating

beforeUpdateupdated

beforeUpdate: the homeowner has requested a wall knocked down and a new room added — contractors are about to start, but nothing's physically changed yet.

updated: the renovation is finished, the paint is dry, and the new layout is fully visible and walkable.

🏚️

Destruction

beforeDestroydestroyed

beforeDestroy: moving-out day — cancel the mail forwarding, shut off the utilities, unplug every appliance (remove event listeners, clear timers) before the movers arrive.

destroyed: the house has been demolished, the lot is empty. Final confirmation that the instance is entirely gone.

Best Practices

Avoid Arrow Functions in Hooks

Real Life

An intercom system that's supposed to auto-connect to whichever house it's installed in — but wiring it with a walkie-talkie borrowed from a house down the street means it stays tuned to that other house's channel instead.

Analogy

Arrow functions inherit this from their surrounding scope rather than binding to the component — exactly like that borrowed walkie-talkie staying tuned to the wrong address.

Fetch in mounted, not created

Real Life

Scheduling a furniture delivery to an address before the house is confirmed built — with server-side rendering, there may be no real "house" (DOM) yet to receive the delivery.

Analogy

Waiting until mounted — the house is physically standing — keeps the delivery compatible whether the house was built on-site (browser) or off-site (SSR).

Non-Blocking async/await

Real Life

Calling the furniture store to schedule a delivery — you don't stand at the door waiting for the truck. You keep living in the house, and the delivery arrives whenever it's ready.

Analogy

An async call inside mounted doesn't freeze the house's rendering while the "delivery truck" (API call) is en route.

App vs. Component Lifecycle

Real Life

An entire apartment building (the app) going through construction and demolition, while each individual unit (a component) has its own move-in and move-out day nested inside that timeline.

Analogy

A single tenant moving out doesn't demolish the building — their unit just runs its own destruction hooks while the rest of the building carries on.

Every Vue component lives the same four chapters a house does: built, moved into, renovated, and eventually torn down — the hooks are just where you're allowed to step in at each chapter.
