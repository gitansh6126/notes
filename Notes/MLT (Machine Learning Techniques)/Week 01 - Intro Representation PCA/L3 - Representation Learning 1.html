Representation
Learning: Foundations

— why "understanding" a dataset really means finding the shortest way to describe it —

"Comprehension is Compression"

The goal of unsupervised learning is to take data points living in high-dimensional space and find a smaller, more efficient way to represent them — without losing what matters.

Real Life

A great book review that captures the entire plot, themes, and ending in three sentences.

Analogy

If you truly understand something, you can summarize it. A student who can only repeat the textbook word-for-word hasn't compressed the idea — they haven't understood it.

Efficient Representation

If your data follows a pattern, you don't need to store every point in full detail — just the pattern, plus a small note per point.

One Representative + Coefficients

Real Life

A tailor keeps one master pattern for a shirt, then stores just a single "scale factor" per customer (small, medium, large, or an exact size number).

Analogy

Instead of cutting a brand-new pattern for every customer, the tailor reuses one shape and just rescales it — exactly like storing one representative vector plus one coefficient per data point.

Linear Trend = Cheap Storage

Real Life

A delivery company tracking trucks that all drive the same highway — it only needs to log each truck's distance along the road, not its full GPS coordinates.

Analogy

The highway is the "representative line"; each truck's position is just one number (distance travelled) instead of two (latitude and longitude).

Exact vs. Approximate Reconstruction

Exact Reconstruction

When: every data point lies perfectly on the line.

Real life: a train that only ever moves along its fixed track — knowing its distance from the station tells you its exact position, no error at all.

Approximate Reconstruction

When: points don't align — you either add more representatives (costlier) or accept some error.

Real life: a car driving near, but not exactly on, a mapped road — GPS approximates your position by snapping you to the nearest point on the road.

Projection: Finding the Best Proxy

Projecting onto a Line

Real Life

The shadow a tree casts on the ground at noon.

Analogy

The shadow is a "compressed" 1D version of the full 3D tree — it loses some information (height detail), but keeps the most important part (position along the ground).

Minimizing the Error

Real Life

A carpenter fitting a shelf to a slightly uneven wall — sanding it down so the gap is as small as possible everywhere, not perfect in one spot and terrible elsewhere.

Analogy

This mirrors minimizing the squared length of the error vector — spreading the "misfit" out evenly rather than letting it spike anywhere.

The Formula, Plainly

c* = xᵀ w‖w‖

The best coefficient c* is how far x "leans into" the direction of w, scaled by w's own length.

if ‖w‖ = 1  →   c* = xᵀ w

Give the representative w a "unit length," and the messy division disappears — c* is just the dot product of x and w.

Intuition

Real Life

Measuring how much of your walk was "forward" using a compass — you don't need your full zig-zag path, just the component pointing in the forward direction.

Analogy

The compass direction is w (unit length); how many steps you took "in that direction" is exactly c* — a dot product, nothing more exotic.

Representation learning is the art of finding the shortest honest description of your data — and projection is how you measure how much gets kept when you shrink it.
