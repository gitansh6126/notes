01

Mean Centering

Mean centering shifts the data cloud so that its center of mass sits exactly at the origin. Every later step — variance, covariance, PCA — assumes the data is already centered.

Equation (1)
Mean Centering
$$\mathbf{x}'_i = \mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\mu} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{x}_i$$
Subtract the mean from every data point — the centered cloud has zero mean
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The centered version of data point $\mathbf{x}_i$, denoted $\mathbf{x}'_i$, equals the original point minus the mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$. The mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is the average of all $n$ data points, computed feature-by-feature.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{x}_i$ The $i$-th original data point $\mathbf{x}_1 = [170,\; 65]^{\raisebox{-1.5pt}{\tiny T}}$
$\boldsymbol{\mu}$ Mean vector (one entry per feature) $\boldsymbol{\mu} = [175,\; 70]^{\raisebox{-1.5pt}{\tiny T}}$
$\mathbf{x}'_i$ Centered data point — sum of all $\mathbf{x}'_i$ is $\mathbf{0}$ $\mathbf{x}'_1 = [-5,\; -5]^{\raisebox{-1.5pt}{\tiny T}}$
$n$ Number of data points (samples) $n = 5$
$\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}$ Sum over all $n$ observations $i = 1, 2, \dots, n$
▶ Intuition
Imagine a cluster of cars parked in a lot. Mean centering is like moving the entire cluster so that the average parking spot is exactly at a lamp post marked $0$. The cars' positions relative to each other don't change — only the whole cluster shifts. This removes the location while keeping the shape.

After centering, the data is ready for variance and covariance calculations, which describe the shape without being distracted by where the cluster sits.
▶ Step-by-Step
1
Identify each feature (dimension) of the data separately
$\text{Feature } j$
↓
2
Compute the mean $\mu_j$ of feature $j$ across all $n$ points
$\displaystyle \mu_j = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{ij}$
↓
3
Subtract $\mu_j$ from every value in feature $j$
$x'_{ij} = x_{ij} - \mu_j$
↓
4
Verify: the centered values sum to exactly $0$
$\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{n} x'_{ij} = 0$
▶ Visual: Before and After Centering
The entire cloud shifts so that the mean lands at the origin. Distances between points are preserved.
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: One feature with values $[4,\; 6,\; 8,\; 10,\; 12]$.
$i$$x_i$$x_i - \mu$$x'_i$
1$4$$4 - 8$$-4$
2$6$$6 - 8$$-2$
3$8$$8 - 8$$0$
4$10$$10 - 8$$2$
5$12$$12 - 8$$4$
$\mu = \frac{4 + 6 + 8 + 10 + 12}{5} = \mathbf{8}$
$\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{5} x'_i = (-4) + (-2) + 0 + 2 + 4 = \mathbf{0}$
i
What centering achieves

The original data had a mean of $8$. After subtracting $8$, the new mean is $0$. The spread is unchanged: the smallest value moved from $4$ to $-4$ and the largest from $12$ to $4$, preserving the same range of $8$ units. This is exactly what PCA needs: variance calculations are simpler when the mean is zero.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Centering along the wrong axis: Subtract a row mean from a column (or vice versa). Each feature gets its own mean subtracted.

✖ Using one global mean: If you have 3 features, you must compute 3 separate means — not one average for everything.

✖ Confusing with standardization: Centering only subtracts $\mu$. Standardization also divides by $\sigma$.

→
Quick check

After centering, the values must sum to exactly zero. Always verify this before moving to variance or covariance.

02

Variance

Variance measures how spread out a single feature is — the average squared distance from the mean. It is the diagonal of the covariance matrix.

Equation (2)
Population Variance
$$\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)^2$$
The average squared deviation from the mean
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The variance $\sigma^2$ equals one over $n$ times the sum over $i$ from $1$ to $n$ of the squared difference between each value $x_i$ and the mean $\mu$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\sigma^2$ Variance (population) $\sigma^2 = 2.67$
$x_i$ The $i$-th value of a single feature $x_1 = 4$
$\mu$ Mean of that feature $\mu = 8$
$n$ Number of observations $n = 5$
alt form $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[x^2] - (\mathbb{E}[x])^2$ Often faster with integers
▶ Intuition
Variance answers: "on average, how far do points wander from the center?" Squaring the distances gives extra weight to far-away points — a single outlier at distance $10$ contributes $100$ to the sum, while ten points at distance $1$ contribute only $10$ combined.

If variance is large, the data is spread wide. If it is small, the data hugs the mean tightly.
▶ Step-by-Step
1
Compute the mean $\mu$ of the feature
$\displaystyle \mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$
↓
2
Find each deviation from the mean: $x_i - \mu$
$d_i = x_i - \mu$
↓
3
Square every deviation: $(x_i - \mu)^2$
$d_i^2$
↓
4
Average the squared deviations
$\displaystyle \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i^2$
▶ Visual: Squared Deviations
The squared deviation $(x_i - \mu)^2$ is the area of a square whose side is the distance from $x_i$ to $\mu$. Points farther from the mean produce much larger squares.
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: Values $[2,\; 4,\; 6]$ (population variance).
$i$$x_i$$x_i - \mu$$(x_i - \mu)^2$
1$2$$-2$$4$
2$4$$0$$0$
3$6$$2$$4$
$\mu = \frac{2+4+6}{3} = 4$
$\displaystyle \sigma^2 = \frac{4 + 0 + 4}{3} = \frac{8}{3} = \mathbf{2.6667}$
i
What this value means

A variance of $2.67$ means the average squared distance from the mean is $2.67$. The standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{2.67} \approx 1.63$ is in the original units and tells us that a typical point is about $1.63$ units from the center.

If we had used the alt formula: $\mathbb{E}[x^2] = (4 + 16 + 36)/3 = 18.67$, $(\mathbb{E}[x])^2 = 16$, $\sigma^2 = 18.67 - 16 = 2.67$ — same result.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Not squaring deviations: Positive and negative deviations would cancel out, wrongly suggesting zero spread.

✖ Dividing by $n$ vs $n-1$: Population variance uses $n$; sample variance uses $n-1$. Check what the question asks.

✖ Reporting $\sigma$ when $\sigma^2$ was asked: They are different quantities (standard deviation vs. variance).

→
Shortcut

Use $\sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[x^2] - (\mathbb{E}[x])^2$ when numbers are small integers — it avoids computing every deviation individually.

03

Pairwise Covariance

Covariance measures how two features move together. Positive means they rise and fall in sync; negative means one goes up when the other goes down.

Equation (3)
Pairwise Covariance
$$\operatorname{cov}(x, y) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu_x)(y_i - \mu_y)$$
The average product of deviations — the off-diagonal engine of the covariance matrix
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The covariance between $x$ and $y$ equals one over $n$ times the sum over $i$ from $1$ to $n$ of the product of the deviation of $x_i$ from $\mu_x$ and the deviation of $y_i$ from $\mu_y$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\operatorname{cov}(x, y)$ Covariance between feature $x$ and feature $y$ $\operatorname{cov}(x, y) = 2.5$
$x_i, y_i$ Paired observations of two features $x_1 = 1,\; y_1 = 2$
$\mu_x, \mu_y$ Means of $x$ and $y$ respectively $\mu_x = 2.5,\; \mu_y = 5$
alt form $\operatorname{cov}(x, y) = \mathbb{E}[xy] - \mathbb{E}[x]\mathbb{E}[y]$ Avoids writing deviations
symmetry $\operatorname{cov}(x, y) = \operatorname{cov}(y, x)$ always Mirror across diagonal
▶ Intuition
When both features are above their means together ($x_i > \mu_x$ and $y_i > \mu_y$), the product $(x_i - \mu_x)(y_i - \mu_y)$ is positive. When both are below together, the product is also positive (two negatives multiply to a positive). When one is above and the other below, the product is negative.

Adding up all these products and averaging tells you the net direction of the relationship: positive, negative, or near zero.
▶ Step-by-Step
1
Compute means $\mu_x$ and $\mu_y$
$\mu_x,\; \mu_y$
↓
2
Compute both deviations: $x_i - \mu_x$ and $y_i - \mu_y$
$d_i^{(x)},\; d_i^{(y)}$
↓
3
Multiply the paired deviations
$d_i^{(x)} \cdot d_i^{(y)}$
↓
4
Sum all $n$ products and divide by $n$
$\displaystyle \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i^{(x)} d_i^{(y)}$
▶ Visual: Positive vs Negative Covariance
Left: as $x$ increases, $y$ increases (positive covariance). Right: as $x$ increases, $y$ decreases (negative covariance).
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: $x = [1, 2, 3, 4]$, $y = [2, 4, 6, 8]$.
$i$$x_i$$y_i$$x_i - \mu_x$$y_i - \mu_y$product
1$1$$2$$-1.5$$-3$$4.5$
2$2$$4$$-0.5$$-1$$0.5$
3$3$$6$$0.5$$1$$0.5$
4$4$$8$$1.5$$3$$4.5$
$\mu_x = 2.5$, $\mu_y = 5$, $\sum \text{products} = 4.5 + 0.5 + 0.5 + 4.5 = 10$
$\displaystyle \operatorname{cov}(x, y) = \frac{10}{4} = \mathbf{2.5}$
i
What this value means

A covariance of $2.5$ is positive, confirming that $x$ and $y$ move in the same direction. The magnitude is moderate: to judge strength we would compute the correlation $\rho = \operatorname{cov}(x,y) / (\sigma_x \sigma_y)$.

Note that $\operatorname{cov}(x, x) = \sigma_x^2$: the diagonal of the covariance matrix is just the variance of each feature.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Mixing up which mean goes where: Always subtract $\mu_x$ from $x_i$ and $\mu_y$ from $y_i$.

✖ Sign errors: $(-2) \times (-3) = +6$, not $-6$. Two negatives produce a positive product.

✖ Forgetting symmetry: $\operatorname{cov}(x, y) = \operatorname{cov}(y, x)$ — don't compute both separately.

04

Covariance Matrix

The covariance matrix collects all pairwise covariances into a single $d \times d$ structure. It is the central object of PCA.

Equation (4)
Covariance Matrix (Matrix Form)
$$\mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n}\,\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}, \qquad \mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times n} \text{ (already centered)}$$
The outer product of the centered data matrix with itself, scaled by $1/n$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$ equals one over $n$ times the centered data matrix $\mathbf{X}$ multiplied by its transpose $\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}$. Each entry $(i, j)$ of $\mathbf{C}$ is $\operatorname{cov}(\text{feature } i, \text{feature } j)$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{C}$ $d \times d$ covariance matrix, symmetric $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}$
$\mathbf{X}$ $d \times n$ centered data matrix $2$ features, $4$ points
$\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}$ $n \times d$ transpose of $\mathbf{X}$ Used to form $d \times d$ product
$C_{ii}$ Variance of feature $i$ (diagonal) $C_{11} = \sigma_1^2$
$C_{ij}$ Covariance between feature $i$ and $j$ $C_{12} = \operatorname{cov}(1,2)$
▶ Intuition
The covariance matrix is a "friendship map" between features. The diagonal entries tell you how much each feature varies alone (its variance). The off-diagonal entries tell you whether pairs of features rise and fall together (positive), move oppositely (negative), or are unrelated ($\approx 0$).

PCA will later find the eigenvectors of $\mathbf{C}$ — the directions of maximum variance in the feature space.
▶ Visual: Covariance Matrix Structure
Diagonal = variances (green), off-diagonal = covariances (gold). The matrix is always symmetric.
▶ Numerical Example ($d = 2$, $n = 4$)
Setup: Feature A $[1,3,5,7]$, Feature B $[2,4,6,8]$. Data is already centered.
Centered Data $\mathbf{X}$ ($2 \times 4$)
PointFeature A'Feature B'
1$-3$$-3$
2$-1$$-1$
3$1$$1$
4$3$$3$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{XX}^{\!\top} = \begin{pmatrix} 9+1+1+9 & 9+1+1+9 \\ 9+1+1+9 & 9+1+1+9 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 20 & 20 \\ 20 & 20 \end{pmatrix}$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{4}\begin{pmatrix} 20 & 20 \\ 20 & 20 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 5 \\ 5 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$
i
What this matrix tells us

Both features have variance $5$ (diagonal). The covariance is also $5$ — a very strong relationship (in fact, the features are perfectly correlated). The matrix is symmetric. PCA will use this matrix to find the principal components.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Forgetting to center first: $\mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{XX}^{\!\top}$ requires $\mathbf{X}$ to be mean-centered already.

✖ Confusing $\mathbf{XX}^{\!\top}$ with $\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}\mathbf{X}$: The first is $d \times d$ (covariance), the second is $n \times n$ (Gram matrix).

✖ Sign errors: Double-check every product in $\mathbf{XX}^{\!\top}$, especially when negative values are involved.

05

Standardization ($z$-score)

Standardization rescales a feature to have zero mean and unit variance. It is the standard pre-processing step before PCA.

Equation (5)
Standardization ($z$-Score)
$$z_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma}$$
Centering then scaling by standard deviation — result has mean $0$, std $1$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The $z$-score of $x_i$ equals the difference between $x_i$ and the mean $\mu$, divided by the standard deviation $\sigma$. A $z$-score tells you how many standard deviations above or below the mean a point is.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$z_i$ Standardized value ($z$-score) $z_1 = -1.34$
$x_i$ Original value $x_1 = 2$
$\mu$ Mean of the feature $\mu = 5$
$\sigma$ Standard deviation of the feature (not variance) $\sigma \approx 2.236$
▶ Intuition
Standardization is like grading on a curve: it tells you where a student stands relative to the class average, measured in "number of standard deviations." A $z$-score of $+2$ means the value is two standard deviations above average — regardless of the original units (centimeters, dollars, seconds, etc.).

Before PCA, standardization is essential when features are measured in different units. Without it, a feature measured in kilometers (with large numbers) would dominate a feature measured in millimeters (with small numbers), purely due to scale.
▶ Step-by-Step
1
Compute the mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$
$\mu,\; \sigma$
↓
2
Center: subtract the mean $x_i - \mu$
$x_i - \mu$
↓
3
Scale: divide by $\sigma$
$\displaystyle z_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma}$
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: Standardize $[2, 4, 6, 8]$ — $\mu = 5$, $\sigma \approx 2.236$.
$i$$x_i$$x_i - \mu$$(x_i - \mu) / \sigma$$z_i$
1$2$$-3$$-3 / 2.236$$-1.34$
2$4$$-1$$-1 / 2.236$$-0.45$
3$6$$1$$1 / 2.236$$0.45$
4$8$$3$$3 / 2.236$$1.34$
$\displaystyle \text{Mean}(z) = 0,\qquad \text{Std}(z) = 1$
i
What standardized values mean

A $z$-score of $-1.34$ means the value is $1.34$ standard deviations below the mean. A $z$-score of $+1.34$ is $1.34$ standard deviations above. About $95\%$ of $z$-scores fall between $-2$ and $+2$ for roughly normal data.

After standardization, all features are on the same scale, so PCA treats them fairly.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Dividing by variance instead of standard deviation: $z = (x - \mu) / \sigma$, not $(x - \mu) / \sigma^2$.

✖ Confusing standardization with normalization: Standardization produces $z$-scores (mean $0$, std $1$), not values in $[0, 1]$.

✖ Skipping centering: Standardization always subtracts $\mu$ first, then divides by $\sigma$.

06

Normalization (Min-Max Scaling)

Normalization rescales a feature to a fixed range, typically $[0, 1]$, by using the minimum and maximum values.

Equation (6)
Normalization (Min-Max Scaling)
$$x'_i = \frac{x_i - \min(x)}{\max(x) - \min(x)}$$
Shifts and compresses the data to lie in $[0, 1]$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The normalized value $x'_i$ equals the difference between $x_i$ and the minimum value, divided by the range (maximum minus minimum). The smallest value becomes $0$, the largest becomes $1$, and everything else falls proportionally in between.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$x'_i$ Normalized value in $[0, 1]$ $x'_1 = 0$
$\min(x)$ Minimum value of the feature $2$
$\max(x)$ Maximum value of the feature $8$
range $\max(x) - \min(x)$ (denominator) $6$
▶ Intuition
Normalization is like finding where you rank on a scale from shortest to tallest. If you're the shortest person, you're at $0$; the tallest is at $1$; everyone else is in between. It does not preserve the shape of the distribution as faithfully as standardization, because a single outlier can compress everyone else into a tiny sliver of the $[0,1]$ range.

Normalization is rarely used before PCA, but it is common in neural network preprocessing and when the algorithm requires bounded inputs ($[0, 1]$).
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: Normalize $[2, 4, 6, 8]$ — $\min = 2$, $\max = 8$, range $= 6$.
$i$$x_i$$x_i - \min$$(x_i - \min) / \text{range}$$x'_i$
1$2$$0$$0 / 6$$0.00$
2$4$$2$$2 / 6$$0.33$
3$6$$4$$4 / 6$$0.67$
4$8$$6$$6 / 6$$1.00$
$\displaystyle \min(x') = 0,\qquad \max(x') = 1$
i
Comparing standardization vs. normalization for the same data

For $[2, 4, 6, 8]$:
Standardized: $[-1.34,\; -0.45,\; 0.45,\; 1.34]$ (mean $0$, std $1$)
Normalized: $[0,\; 0.33,\; 0.67,\; 1.00]$ (range $[0, 1]$)

Both rescale, but they produce different numbers. Standardization is preferred before PCA because it preserves the relative spread (variance) structure.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Using the terms interchangeably: Standardization and normalization are different operations with different formulas and properties.

✖ Outlier sensitivity: A single extreme value can compress all normalized values into a tiny sub-interval of $[0, 1]$.

✖ Applying normalization before PCA: PCA assumes comparable variance scales — standardization is the correct choice when features have different units.

→
Memory anchor

Standardize → Std = 1. Normalize → [0,1] raNge.

07

Correlation Matrix vs Covariance Matrix

The correlation matrix is a scale-free version of the covariance matrix. Every entry is in $[-1, 1]$, making it easy to compare relationship strengths across different pairs of features.

Equation (7)
Correlation Matrix
$$\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{D}^{-1}\,\mathbf{C}\,\mathbf{D}^{-1}, \qquad \mathbf{D} = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \dots, \sigma_d)$$
Divide every entry $C_{ij}$ by $\sigma_i \sigma_j$ — diagonal becomes $1$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The correlation matrix $\mathbf{R}$ equals the diagonal matrix of standard deviations $\mathbf{D}$, inverted, multiplied by the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$, then multiplied by $\mathbf{D}^{-1}$ again. Entry $(i, j)$ of $\mathbf{R}$ is $\displaystyle \frac{C_{ij}}{\sigma_i \sigma_j}$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{R}$ Correlation matrix — $R_{ii} = 1$ $\mathbf{R} \in [-1, 1]^{d \times d}$
$\mathbf{C}$ Covariance matrix (needs $\sigma_i$ to normalize) $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$
$\mathbf{D}$ Diagonal matrix of standard deviations $\mathbf{D} = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_d)$
$\sigma_i$ Standard deviation of feature $i$ $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{C_{11}}$
▶ Intuition
Covariance tells you the direction of a relationship, but its size depends on the units. A covariance of $1000$ sounds huge in meters but might be $0.001$ when converted to kilometers — for the same relationship.

Correlation strips the units away, always landing in $[-1, 1]$. A correlation of $+1$ means perfect positive linear relationship, $-1$ means perfect negative, and $0$ means no linear relationship. This makes it possible to compare relationship strength across any pair of features.
▶ Visual: Correlation Strength Spectrum
Correlation coefficients range from $-1$ (perfect negative) through $0$ (none) to $+1$ (perfect positive).
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: Given $\mathbf{C} = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 9 & 3 \\ -1 & 3 & 1\end{pmatrix}$.
$i$$j$$C_{ij}$$\sigma_i$$\sigma_j$$R_{ij} = C_{ij} / (\sigma_i \sigma_j)$
$1$$1$$4$$2$$2$$4 / 4 = 1$
$1$$2$$2$$2$$3$$2 / 6 = 0.33$
$1$$3$$-1$$2$$1$$-1 / 2 = -0.5$
$2$$3$$3$$3$$1$$3 / 3 = 1.0$
$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = [\sqrt{4},\; \sqrt{9},\; \sqrt{1}] = [2,\; 3,\; 1]$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{R} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0.33 & -0.5 \\ 0.33 & 1 & 1.0 \\ -0.5 & 1.0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$
i
What the correlation matrix reveals

The correlation between features 2 and 3 is $1.0$ — a perfect linear relationship. Feature 1 and feature 3 have a correlation of $-0.5$ — a moderate negative relationship. The diagonal is always $1$ because every feature is perfectly correlated with itself.

Unlike the covariance matrix, these numbers are independent of the original measurement units, so you can fairly compare the strength of any pair.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Dividing by variance instead of std deviation: Correlation uses $\sigma$, not $\sigma^2$.

✖ Diagonal not equal to 1: Every $R_{ii}$ must be exactly $1$. If it is not, the calculation is wrong.

✖ Treating large covariance as "strong": A large covariance does not imply a strong relationship — it depends on the units. Always check the correlation.

→
Quick check

$\mathbf{R}$ is symmetric and every off-diagonal entry lies in $[-1, 1]$. The diagonal is always $1$. If you have $\mathbf{C}$ and the standard deviations, you can reconstruct $\mathbf{R}$ — and vice versa.
