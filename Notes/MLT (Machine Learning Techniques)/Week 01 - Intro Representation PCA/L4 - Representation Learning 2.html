PCA & Eigenvectors

— finding the one direction that keeps the most truth about your data —

Continuing from L03 Same shadow-and-projection idea — now we ask: which angle for the "light" gives the least distorted shadow?

Real data never sits perfectly on a line.

So instead of assuming a perfect fit, we search for the line that loses the least information when every point is squashed onto it — minimizing reconstruction error.

Real Life

Photographing a handful of scattered marbles on a table. Shoot from directly above and they look like a tight, overlapping blob. Shoot from the angle where they're most spread out, and you can tell them apart.

Analogy

The "best angle" photo is the one that keeps the most distinguishing information — exactly what PCA searches for when picking a line to project onto.

Why Minimizing Error = Maximizing Spread

These sound like opposite goals, but they're mathematically the same target.

The line that keeps points furthest apart from each other is, automatically, the line closest to every point.

Real Life

A tailor stretching a single elastic band across a rack of differently-sized shirts so it touches as many shirt shoulders as possible.

Analogy

The band that reaches the widest spread of shoulders (max variance) is also, geometrically, the band sitting closest to all of them on average (min error) — same line, two ways of describing it.

The Covariance Matrix

Covariance Matrix (C)

Averages the outer products of every data point — a table of how features move together.

Real Life

An ice cream shop's sales log showing that ice cream sales and sunscreen sales rise and fall together — while ice cream sales and umbrella sales move in opposite directions.

Analogy

C is that whole sales log condensed into one grid — every pair of "features" (ice cream, sunscreen, umbrellas) gets a number for how much they co-move.

Eigenvectors & Eigenvalues

Eigenvector (Direction)

The direction along which the data spreads out the most.

Real Life

Fitting the smallest oval around a cluster of stars in the night sky — the oval has one long axis and one short axis.

Analogy

That long axis is the top eigenvector — the "main direction" the star cluster stretches in, even though individual stars sit off to either side.

Eigenvalue (Amount)

How much variance lies along that eigenvector's direction.

Real Life

How long the oval's long axis actually is, compared to its short axis.

Analogy

A long, thin oval (big eigenvalue) means the data has one dominant direction of spread worth keeping; a nearly circular oval (small eigenvalue) means there's no real "best angle" to project onto.

The Formula, Plainly

maximize  wᵀ C w   subject to   ‖w‖² = 1

Find the unit direction w that makes the "spread formula" wᵀCw as large as possible.

C = (1/n) ∑ xᵢ xᵢᵀ

C is just the average of every point "multiplied by itself" — the co-movement table above, written in matrix form.

best w = eigenvector of C with the largest eigenvalue

This optimization has a shortcut: no trial-and-error searching needed — the answer is exactly the top eigenvector of C.

PCA doesn't guess at the best line by trial and error — it reads it straight off the covariance matrix, as the eigenvector holding the most spread.
