01

Eigenvectors

An eigenvector of a matrix is a special non-zero vector that, when the matrix multiplies it, only stretches or compresses it — it does not change direction.

Equation (1)
Eigenvalue Equation
$$\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda\,\mathbf{v}$$
Multiplying $\mathbf{v}$ by $\mathbf{A}$ just scales $\mathbf{v}$ by $\lambda$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“For a square matrix $\mathbf{A}$, a vector $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector if multiplying $\mathbf{A}$ by $\mathbf{v}$ produces a result that is a scalar multiple of $\mathbf{v}$ itself. The scalar $\lambda$ is the corresponding eigenvalue.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{A}$ A $d \times d$ square matrix $\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\1 & 2\end{pmatrix}$
$\mathbf{v}$ Eigenvector — direction stays the same $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$
$\lambda$ Eigenvalue — how much $\mathbf{v}$ is scaled $\lambda = 3$
$\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$ Eigenvectors are always non-zero Zero vector is excluded
▶ Intuition
Imagine you have a square rubber sheet. When you shear it with a transformation matrix, most vectors change their direction. But certain special directions — the eigenvectors — simply get stretched or compressed along their own line.

An eigenvector says: "I know which way to point so that the transformation only changes my length, not my direction." The eigenvalue $\lambda$ tells you the new length relative to the original.
▶ Visual: Eigenvector vs Non-Eigenvector
Left: $\mathbf{v}$ stays on the same line after $\mathbf{A}$ acts on it (eigenvector). Right: $\mathbf{w}$ rotates off its original line.
▶ Quick Verification
Given: $\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\1 & 2\end{pmatrix}$, $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$, $\lambda = 3$.
$\displaystyle \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1\\1 & 2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\3\end{pmatrix} = 3\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ ✓
$\mathbf{v} = [1,1]^{\!\top}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda = 3$
!
Common Mistakes

✖ Any scalar multiple is also an eigenvector: If $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector, so is $2\mathbf{v}$, $-3\mathbf{v}$, etc. We typically normalize eigenvectors to unit length.

✖ The zero vector is never an eigenvector: The definition requires $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$, because $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$ always holds trivially.

✖ Eigenvectors are not unique: The direction matters, not the length. Two eigenvectors with the same eigenvalue span a subspace.

02

Eigenvalues

The eigenvalue $\lambda$ measures how much the eigenvector is scaled. Positive eigenvalues mean stretch; negative mean reversal; zero means the matrix collapses that direction entirely.

Equation (2)
Eigenvalue Meaning
$$\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i\,\mathbf{v}_i, \qquad i = 1, 2, \dots, d$$
A $d \times d$ matrix has at most $d$ eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The $i$-th eigenvalue $\lambda_i$ and its eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_i$ satisfy $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}_i = \lambda_i \mathbf{v}_i$. A $d \times d$ matrix has exactly $d$ eigenvalues (counting multiplicity and including complex values), each paired with its own eigenvector.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\lambda_i$ The $i$-th eigenvalue (a scalar) $\lambda_1 = 3$
$\mathbf{v}_i$ The $i$-th eigenvector (a direction) $\mathbf{v}_1 = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1,1]^{\!\top}$
$\lambda > 0$ Eigenvector is stretched (same direction) $\lambda = 3$ → tripled
$\lambda < 0$ Eigenvector is reversed and scaled
$\lambda = 0$ Matrix collapses that direction — $\mathbf{A}$ is singular Matrix not invertible
▶ Intuition
Think of the eigenvalues as the "power ratings" of each eigenvector direction. A large eigenvalue means the matrix amplifies that direction strongly. A small eigenvalue means it barely affects it.

In PCA, the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix tell you how much variance each principal component captures. The largest eigenvalue corresponds to the direction of maximum spread in the data.
$\lambda_1 = 5$

Large eigenvalue

Strong stretch — high variance direction

$\lambda_2 = 0.3$

Small eigenvalue

Weak stretch — low variance direction

$\lambda_3 = 0$

Zero eigenvalue

No variance — data lies on a lower subspace

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Eigenvalues can be negative: Unlike variances (which are always $\geq 0$), eigenvalues of a general matrix can be negative. For symmetric matrices like covariance matrices, they are always non-negative.

✖ Eigenvalues can be complex: Non-symmetric matrices may have complex eigenvalues. PCA only deals with real, non-negative eigenvalues because covariance matrices are symmetric.

✖ Counting eigenvalues: A $d \times d$ matrix has exactly $d$ eigenvalues (with multiplicity), even if some are equal (repeated).

03

Characteristic Equation

To find the eigenvalues, we solve the characteristic equation — a polynomial whose roots are the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{A}$.

Equation (3)
Characteristic Equation
$$\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\,\mathbf{I}) = 0$$
The eigenvalues are the roots of this degree-$d$ polynomial in $\lambda$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“Subtract $\lambda$ from each diagonal entry of $\mathbf{A}$ (by forming $\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}$), then compute the determinant. Set the result equal to zero. The values of $\lambda$ that satisfy this equation are the eigenvalues.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\det(\cdot)$ Determinant of a square matrix $\det\begin{pmatrix}a & b\\c & d\end{pmatrix} = ad-bc$
$\mathbf{I}$ Identity matrix ($1$s on diagonal, $0$s elsewhere) $\mathbf{I}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$
$\lambda\mathbf{I}$ Scalar $\lambda$ times identity — puts $\lambda$ on diagonal $\lambda\mathbf{I} = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda & 0\\0 & \lambda\end{pmatrix}$
▶ Intuition
The equation $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ can be rewritten as $(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$. For a non-zero solution $\mathbf{v}$ to exist, the matrix $(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})$ must be singular (non-invertible). A matrix is singular exactly when its determinant is zero — hence $\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = 0$.
▶ Step-by-Step: 2 × 2 Case
1
Start with $\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix}a & b\\c & d\end{pmatrix}$
$\mathbf{A}$
↓
2
Form $\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}$
$\begin{pmatrix}a-\lambda & b\\c & d-\lambda\end{pmatrix}$
↓
3

$(a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc$
↓
4
Set equal to zero and solve for $\lambda$
$\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad-bc) = 0$
→
Shortcut for 2 × 2

For $\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix}a & b\\c & d\end{pmatrix}$, the characteristic polynomial is $\lambda^2 - \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A})\lambda + \det(\mathbf{A}) = 0$, where $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = a + d$ is the trace.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Computing $\det(\mathbf{A}) - \lambda$ instead of $\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I})$: The $\lambda$ must be subtracted from each diagonal entry before taking the determinant.

✖ Forgetting the identity matrix: $\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}$ means subtracting $\lambda$ only from the diagonal, not from every entry.

✖ Sign errors in the quadratic formula: Double-check the signs when expanding $(a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc$.

04

Finding Eigenpairs (Worked Example)

A complete worked example: given a $2 \times 2$ matrix, find both eigenvalues from the characteristic equation, then solve for the corresponding eigenvectors.

▶ Step 1: Find Eigenvalues
Matrix: $\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 2\\1 & 3\end{pmatrix}$
Characteristic equation:
$\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - (2)(1) = 0$
$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12 - 2 = 0$
$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$
$(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$
$\lambda_1 = 5, \qquad \lambda_2 = 2$
▶ Step 2: Find Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 5$
Solve $(\mathbf{A} - 5\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$:
$\begin{pmatrix}4-5 & 2\\1 & 3-5\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-1 & 2\\1 & -2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}$
Both rows give: $-v_1 + 2v_2 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; v_1 = 2v_2$
$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$ → normalized: $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix}$
▶ Step 3: Find Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 2$
Solve $(\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$:
$\begin{pmatrix}4-2 & 2\\1 & 3-2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 2\\1 & 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v_1\\v_2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\end{pmatrix}$
Both rows give: $v_1 + v_2 = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; v_1 = -v_2$
$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$ → normalized: $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_2 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\\-1\end{pmatrix}$
i
Verification

Check: $\mathbf{A}\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 2\\1 & 3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2/\sqrt{5}\\1/\sqrt{5}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}10/\sqrt{5}\\5/\sqrt{5}\end{pmatrix} = 5\begin{pmatrix}2/\sqrt{5}\\1/\sqrt{5}\end{pmatrix} = \lambda_1\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1$ ✓

The two eigenvectors are orthogonal (perpendicular): $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1 \cdot \hat{\mathbf{v}}_2 = \frac{2}{\sqrt{10}} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{10}} = 0$. This is guaranteed for symmetric matrices by the spectral theorem.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Setting the wrong row to zero: After subtracting $\lambda\mathbf{I}$, both rows should give the same equation (for a non-defective matrix). Use the simpler row.

✖ Forgetting to normalize: Eigenvectors can be scaled arbitrarily. Normalize to unit length for comparisons and for the eigendecomposition.

✖ Confusing $\lambda_1$ with $\mathbf{v}_1$: The eigenvalue is a scalar; the eigenvector is a vector. They always come as a pair.

05

Eigendecomposition

A matrix can be factored into its eigenvectors and eigenvalues. This decomposition reveals the matrix's fundamental action: stretch along each eigenvector direction by the corresponding eigenvalue.

Equation (4)
Eigendecomposition
$$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V}\,\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\,\mathbf{V}^{-1}$$
$\mathbf{V}$ holds eigenvectors as columns, $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is diagonal with eigenvalues
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The matrix $\mathbf{A}$ equals the matrix of eigenvectors $\mathbf{V}$ (columns are $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \dots, \mathbf{v}_d$), multiplied by the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_d)$, multiplied by the inverse of $\mathbf{V}$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{V}$ $d \times d$ matrix; column $i$ is $\mathbf{v}_i$ $\mathbf{V} = [\mathbf{v}_1 \; \mathbf{v}_2]$
$\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ Diagonal matrix; entry $(i,i)$ is $\lambda_i$ $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1 & 0\\0 & \lambda_2\end{pmatrix}$
$\mathbf{V}^{-1}$ Inverse of the eigenvector matrix Exists when eigenvectors are independent
$\mathbf{V}^{-1}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{V}$ = $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ — diagonalization Change of basis to eigenbasis
▶ Intuition
Eigendecomposition says: every linear transformation can be broken into three simple steps:

1. Change basis to the eigenvector directions ($\mathbf{V}^{-1}$)
2. Stretch each direction independently by $\lambda_i$ ($\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$)
3. Change back to the original basis ($\mathbf{V}$)

This is why eigenvectors are so powerful: they turn a complicated matrix operation into independent scalings along perpendicular axes.
▶ Numerical Example ($d = 2$)
From Section 4: $\lambda_1 = 5$, $\mathbf{v}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}[2,1]^{\!\top}$, $\lambda_2 = 2$, $\mathbf{v}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[1,-1]^{\!\top}$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{V} = \begin{pmatrix}2/\sqrt{5} & 1/\sqrt{2}\\1/\sqrt{5} & -1/\sqrt{2}\end{pmatrix}, \quad \boldsymbol{\Lambda} = \begin{pmatrix}5 & 0\\0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 2\\1 & 3\end{pmatrix}$ ✓
$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^{-1}$ reconstructs the original matrix
i
Key property

When $\mathbf{A}$ is symmetric, $\mathbf{V}$ is orthogonal ($\mathbf{V}^{-1} = \mathbf{V}^{\!\top}$), so $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^{\!\top}$. This is exactly the form PCA uses.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Mixing up the order: It is $\mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^{-1}$, not $\mathbf{V}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}$. The eigenvector matrix "sandwiches" the eigenvalue matrix.

✖ Not all matrices are diagonalizable: If a matrix does not have $d$ independent eigenvectors, the decomposition does not exist. Symmetric matrices always are.

✖ Eigenvector order must match eigenvalue order: Column $i$ of $\mathbf{V}$ must correspond to $\lambda_i$ in $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$.

06

Spectral Theorem

The spectral theorem is the result that makes PCA possible. It guarantees that every symmetric matrix has real eigenvalues and orthonormal eigenvectors.

Equation (5)
Spectral Theorem (for Symmetric Matrices)
$$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\,\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\,\mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}, \qquad \mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}$$
Every real symmetric matrix has real eigenvalues and orthonormal eigenvectors
▶ Read the Equation in English
“If $\mathbf{A}$ is a real symmetric matrix ($\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^{\!\top}$), then it can be decomposed as $\mathbf{Q}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}$, where $\mathbf{Q}$ is an orthogonal matrix (its columns are orthonormal eigenvectors) and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is diagonal with real eigenvalues.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningKey Property
$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^{\!\top}$ Symmetric matrix $A_{ij} = A_{ji}$ for all $i,j$
$\mathbf{Q}$ Orthogonal matrix (eigenvectors as columns) $\mathbf{Q}^{-1} = \mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}$
$\mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}$ Columns are orthonormal: unit length and perpendicular $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_i \cdot \hat{\mathbf{v}}_j = \delta_{ij}$
$\lambda_i \in \mathbb{R}$ All eigenvalues are real (no complex numbers) Critical for PCA to work
▶ Intuition
The spectral theorem says symmetric matrices are the "nicest" matrices in linear algebra. They always have:

• Real eigenvalues (no weird rotations or complex scaling)
• Orthogonal eigenvectors (perfectly perpendicular directions)
• An orthonormal eigenbasis (the eigenvectors form a rotated coordinate system)

Since the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$ is always symmetric ($\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{C}^{\!\top}$), the spectral theorem guarantees PCA will work.
▶ Visual: Orthogonal Eigenvectors of a Symmetric Matrix
The eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix are orthogonal (perpendicular). The longer arrow corresponds to the larger eigenvalue.
▶ General Matrix vs Symmetric Matrix
General Matrix
Eigenvalues may be complex
Eigenvectors may not be orthogonal
May not be diagonalizable
$\mathbf{A} \neq \mathbf{A}^{\!\top}$ in general
Symmetric Matrix
Eigenvalues are always real
Eigenvectors are always orthogonal
Always diagonalizable ($\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}$)
$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^{\!\top}$
!
Common Mistakes

✖ The spectral theorem only applies to symmetric matrices: Do not assume eigenvectors are orthogonal for non-symmetric matrices.

✖ Orthonormal vs orthogonal: Orthogonal means perpendicular; orthonormal means perpendicular and unit length. The spectral theorem gives orthonormal eigenvectors after normalization.

✖ The covariance matrix is always symmetric: $\mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}$, and $(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^{\!\top})^{\!\top} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}$, so PCA always satisfies the spectral theorem.

07

Eigenvalues of $\mathbf{C}$ & PCA

The eigenvalues of the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$ directly tell you how much variance each principal component captures. This is the bridge between linear algebra and dimensionality reduction.

Equation (6)
PCA: Eigendecomposition of the Covariance Matrix
$$\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{Q}\,\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\,\mathbf{Q}^{\!\top}, \qquad \text{variance along PC}_i = \lambda_i$$
Eigenvalues of $\mathbf{C}$ = variances along principal component directions
▶ Read the Equation in English
“Decompose the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$ into eigenvectors $\mathbf{Q}$ (the principal component directions) and eigenvalues $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ (the variance along each direction). The largest eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ gives the variance of the first principal component, $\lambda_2$ the second, and so on.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningPCA Role
$\mathbf{C}$ Covariance matrix of centered data $\mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^{\!\top}$
$\hat{\mathbf{v}}_i$ $i$-th eigenvector of $\mathbf{C}$ Direction of $i$-th principal component
$\lambda_i$ $i$-th eigenvalue of $\mathbf{C}$ Variance explained by PC$_i$
$\frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_j \lambda_j}$ Fraction of total variance Percentage explained by PC$_i$
$\sum_{i=1}^{k} \lambda_i$ Total variance captured by top $k$ PCs Choose $k$ for dimensionality reduction
▶ Intuition
PCA is simply this: compute $\mathbf{C}$, find its eigenvalues, sort them from largest to smallest, and keep only the top $k$.

The eigenvalue $\lambda_i$ measures how much "spread" the data has along direction $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_i$. If $\lambda_1 \gg \lambda_2 \gg \lambda_3$, then the data is mostly spread along $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1$, a bit along $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_2$, and almost none along $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_3$. You can safely drop the directions with tiny eigenvalues without losing much information.
▶ Numerical Example: Choosing $k$
Given eigenvalues of $\mathbf{C}$: $\lambda_1 = 4.2$, $\lambda_2 = 1.8$, $\lambda_3 = 0.3$, $\lambda_4 = 0.1$
PC$\lambda_i$Variance %Cumulative %
PC$_1$$4.2$$\frac{4.2}{6.4} = 65.6\%$$65.6\%$
PC$_2$$1.8$$\frac{1.8}{6.4} = 28.1\%$$93.8\%$
PC$_3$$0.3$$\frac{0.3}{6.4} = 4.7\%$$98.4\%$
PC$_4$$0.1$$\frac{0.1}{6.4} = 1.6\%$$100.0\%$
Total variance: $\sum \lambda_i = 4.2 + 1.8 + 0.3 + 0.1 = 6.4$
Keep $k = 2$ PCs: captures $93.8\%$ of total variance
i
Why this works

The total variance in the data equals the sum of all eigenvalues: $\sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C})$. Each eigenvalue tells you what fraction of that total lives along its eigenvector direction. By keeping the top $k$ eigenvalues, you retain the most variance with the fewest dimensions.

The eigenvectors $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_1, \dots, \hat{\mathbf{v}}_k$ become the new axes (principal components), and the data is projected onto these axes to reduce dimensionality.

i
Key property: trace = total variance

$\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C}) = \sum_{i=1}^{d} C_{ii} = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i$. The sum of the diagonal entries equals the sum of the eigenvalues, which equals the total variance.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Eigenvalues are not the same as variance of individual features: The diagonal entries of $\mathbf{C}$ are the feature variances; the eigenvalues are the variances along the rotated principal component axes.

✖ Eigenvalues must be sorted: Always sort $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_d$ before choosing $k$. The numbering is by size, not by the order they were computed.

✖ Small eigenvalues are not zero: A small eigenvalue means low variance, not zero. Only truly zero eigenvalues mean the data is perfectly collinear.

→
PCA in one sentence

Eigendecompose $\mathbf{C}$, sort eigenvalues largest-first, keep the top $k$ eigenvectors as the new axes, and project the data onto them. That is PCA.
