Feature Transformation &
the Limits of PCA

PCA is brilliant at untangling straight-line relationships — and lost the moment your data curves. This lecture unpacks why, and how a clever trick called feature mapping rescues it — plus the price you pay for that rescue.

01

Where PCA Gets Lost: Non-Linear Data

PCA works by rotating the axes to point toward maximum variance — the directions along which points are most spread out. That works beautifully when the "shape" of your data is an elongated cloud. But it has no concept of a curve.

!
The core problem

Give PCA a dataset shaped like a circle, and something breaks: the variance is spread out evenly in every direction. There is no "long axis" to find, so PCA reports that both dimensions matter equally — even though every point on a circle can actually be described by a single number: its angle.

▶ Intuition
Imagine you're standing at the very center of a circular running track, looking outward. No matter which direction you turn, the track is exactly the same distance away. If someone asked you "which direction is the track mainly in?", you couldn't answer — it's equally in every direction. Yet the track itself is a simple, one-dimensional loop; a runner's entire position is described by one number (how far around they've gone). Standing at the center, staring outward in straight lines, is exactly the vantage point PCA is stuck with.
Equation (1)
Circle Equation — Non-Linear Structure
$$x_1^2 + x_2^2 = r^2$$
This relationship is non-linear in $x$ — PCA can't find it because it has no straight direction of maximum curvature
▶ Read the Equation in English
“A point $(x_1, x_2)$ lies on a circle of radius $r$ when the sum of its squared coordinates equals $r^2$. This is a quadratically non-linear relationship — no straight line through the origin can capture it.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$x_1, x_2$ Two coordinates of a data point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ $(3, 4)$
$r$ Radius of the circle $r = 5$
$(x_1, x_2)$ space Original 2D input space $\mathbb{R}^2$
$\theta$ (angle) True 1D intrinsic coordinate $\theta = \arctan(x_2/x_1)$
▶ PCA on Circular Data — Both Axes Look Equally Important
12 points on a circle: PC1 and PC2 capture almost identical variance, so PCA says "keep both" — missing the 1-D angular structure entirely.
i
What this means

PCA isn't "wrong" — it's doing exactly what it's designed to do (maximize captured variance along straight directions). The circle simply has no straight direction to find. We need to change the space the data lives in, not the algorithm.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Thinking PCA is always the right tool: PCA finds linear structure. If your data has curves, circles, or manifolds, PCA's variance-maximization will miss the true underlying dimensionality.

✖ Interpreting equal eigenvalues as "both dimensions matter": Equal eigenvalues on a circle don't mean 2-D structure — they mean the 1-D angular structure is invisible to straight-line thinking.

02

The Fix: Feature Mapping $\varphi$

The trick is to stop looking at the data in its original, low-dimensional space $\mathbb{R}^d$ and instead pass every point through a feature map $\varphi(x)$ that sends it into a much higher-dimensional space $\mathbb{R}^D$, where $D \gg d$. Done well, the curve that was hopelessly non-linear "down here" becomes a flat, linear surface "up there."

Equation (2)
Feature Map Definition
$$\varphi : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^D, \qquad \mathbf{x} \mapsto \varphi(\mathbf{x})$$
A function that lifts each point from $d$-dimensional input space to $D$-dimensional feature space
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The feature map $\varphi$ is a function that takes a $d$-dimensional input vector $\mathbf{x}$ and returns a $D$-dimensional vector $\varphi(\mathbf{x})$. The output space is typically much larger than the input space ($D \gg d$), giving the data more room to unfold into a linear arrangement.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\varphi$ Feature map — lifts data to higher dimension $(x_1, x_2) \mapsto (x_1, x_2, x_1^2 + x_2^2)$
$\mathbb{R}^d$ Original (input) space — $d$ dimensions $d = 2$ for the circle
$\mathbb{R}^D$ Feature (output) space — $D$ dimensions $D = 3$ for the circle map above
$D \gg d$ Feature space much larger than input space $D$ could be millions
▶ Circle Example: Making Non-Linear Structure Linear
1
Start with the circle: $x_1^2 + x_2^2 = r^2$ — non-linear in $\mathbf{x}$
$(x_1, x_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2$
↓
2

$\varphi(x_1, x_2) = (x_1, x_2, x_1^2 + x_2^2)$

PCA in $\mathbb{R}^3$ now sees a flat, linear structure — and finds it easily
✔ Linear subspace found!
▶ Intuition
Think of pizza dough dusted with pepper flakes and shaped into a ring on a flat tray — a "circle" from above. From directly overhead, no flat cut ever separates the ring's pepper flakes from the empty middle. Now push up the center of the dough like a small volcano, so height depends on distance from the middle. Look at the dough from the side: the ring of pepper flakes sits along a slanted rim, and a single flat knife-cut easily separates it from the peak. Nothing about the pepper flakes changed — you just gave the problem one more dimension (height) to live in, and the "impossible" curve became an easy straight cut.
▶ Feature Mapping: Flat & Tangled $\to$ Lifted & Linear
The same data, lifted by $\varphi$: the circular loop in $\mathbb{R}^2$ becomes a linearly separable surface in $\mathbb{R}^D$.
i
The new coordinate isn't magic

The new coordinate $x_1^2 + x_2^2$ is just one extra "ingredient" computed from the originals. Feature mapping is the systematic version of the trick you've probably already used: adding $x^2$ as a feature so a straight-line model can fit a parabola.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Thinking $\varphi$ needs to be known explicitly: As we'll see with the kernel trick, we often only need the dot products $\varphi(x_i)^\mathsf{T}\varphi(x_j)$, not $\varphi(x)$ itself.

✖ Assuming $D$ is small: A polynomial map of degree $p$ with $d$ features produces $D = \binom{d+p}{p}$ dimensions. For $d = 1000, p = 4$, that's roughly $4 \times 10^{13}$ dimensions — completely infeasible to materialize.

03

The Kernel Shortcut

Because mapping increases dimensionality ($D \gg d$), we quickly land in a regime where $D \gg n$ — far more feature dimensions than data samples. Computing a full $D \times D$ covariance matrix of $\varphi(\mathbf{X})$ for PCA becomes infeasible.

Equation (3)
Avoiding the $D \times D$ Covariance: Gram Matrix
$$\mathbf{C}_\varphi = \frac{1}{n} \varphi(\mathbf{X}) \varphi(\mathbf{X})^\mathsf{T} \;\; (D \times D) \quad\longrightarrow\quad \mathbf{K} = \varphi(\mathbf{X})^\mathsf{T} \varphi(\mathbf{X}) \;\; (n \times n)$$
Solve the small $n \times n$ Gram matrix instead — same non-zero eigenvalues
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_\varphi$ in the feature space is $D \times D$, which can be astronomically large. But the Gram matrix $\mathbf{K}$ — whose entries are pairwise dot products $\varphi(x_i)^\mathsf{T}\varphi(x_j)$ — is only $n \times n$. Crucially, both matrices share the same non-zero eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\varphi(\mathbf{X})$ Mapped data matrix ($D \times n$) Each column is $\varphi(x_i)$
$\mathbf{C}_\varphi$ Covariance in feature space ($D \times D$) Often impossible to store
$\mathbf{K}$ Gram (kernel) matrix ($n \times n$) Always small and tractable
$n$ Number of data points $n = 200$
▶ Intuition
It's like being asked to compare every pair of ingredients across 10,000 possible recipes versus comparing your 40 actual dinner guests to each other. Both comparisons can reveal the same insight about who likes what — but one involves a 10,000-row table and the other a 40-row table. You always solve the smaller table if it tells you the same story.
kernel_shortcut.py
# X: n samples, each originally in R^d
phi_X = feature_map(X)            # shape: (n, D)  — D can be huge

# Expensive route (avoid): D x D covariance
cov = (phi_X.T @ phi_X) / n     # shape: (D, D)  — can be 10^15!

# Efficient route: n x n Gram matrix
gram = phi_X @ phi_X.T        # shape: (n, n)  — tiny by comparison
eigvals, eigvecs = eigh(gram)   # solve the small problem
# eigvecs of gram map back to the eigvecs of cov
# we actually needed — same information, far less work
✔
The kernel trick in a nutshell

Instead of computing $\varphi(x)$ explicitly (which may be infinite-dimensional), compute $K(x_i, x_j) = \varphi(x_i)^\mathsf{T}\varphi(x_j)$ directly — a single number. This gives us all the power of the high-dimensional space at $O(n^2)$ cost.

04

The Catch: Exponential Growth

The Gram-matrix shortcut helps with the $n \times n$ computation, but it doesn't rescue us from the underlying problem: if we want our feature map to capture all interactions up to the $p$-th power among $d$ original features, the number of resulting dimensions grows roughly as $O(d^p)$.

Equation (4)
Dimensional Explosion for Polynomial Features
$$D = \binom{d + p}{p} = \frac{(d + p)!}{p! \, d!} = O(d^{\,p})$$
The number of feature dimensions grows as a $p$-th degree polynomial in $d$ — the exponent $p$ is the killer
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The number of dimensions $D$ needed to capture all polynomial interactions up to degree $p$ among $d$ original features is the binomial coefficient $\binom{d+p}{p}$. For fixed $d$, this grows roughly as $d^p$ — each increase in $p$ multiplies the exponent.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$d$ Original number of features $d = 10$
$p$ Maximum degree of interaction $p = 3$ for cubic
$D$ Resulting feature space dimension $\binom{10+3}{3} = 286$
$\binom{d+p}{p}$ Number of combinations with repetition Combinations of $d$ items taken $p$ at a time
▶ Dimensions Needed as Interaction Degree $p$ Grows ($d = 10$)
Bar heights are on a compressed scale — the real jump from $p=10$ to $p=15$ is several orders of magnitude larger than the bars can show. That's the point.
▶ Numerical Example: Dimensional Explosion
Setup: $d = 10$ original features, varying interaction degree $p$.

!
The real problem

Even the "smaller" $n \times n$ trick doesn't save you here — it avoids building the $D \times D$ covariance matrix, but computing $\varphi(x)$ itself still requires materializing all $D$ coordinates for every point, unless the kernel function lets us skip that step entirely.

i
What this means for practice

Brute-force feature mapping is rarely used directly. The polynomial kernel $K(x, y) = (x^\mathsf{T}y + c)^p$ computes the dot product in the $O(d^p)$-dimensional space without ever visiting it — a preview of where kernel methods go next. This is what makes Kernel PCA possible: it gets the benefit of the high-dimensional space at $O(n^3)$ cost.

05

Key Takeaways

PCA only finds straight lines

Data shaped like a circle or curve fools PCA into thinking every direction matters equally — the true 1-D structure is invisible.

Feature mapping lifts data

$\varphi$ maps $\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^D$ where non-linear relationships become linear constraints PCA can find.

Solve the $n \times n$ Gram matrix

When $D \gg n$, solve $\mathbf{K} = \varphi(\mathbf{X})^\mathsf{T}\varphi(\mathbf{X})$ instead of the $D \times D$ covariance — same eigen-information, far less compute.
