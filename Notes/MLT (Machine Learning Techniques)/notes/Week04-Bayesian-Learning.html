Bayesian Learning

Bayesian inference treats parameters as random variables with prior distributions, updating beliefs with observed data through Bayes' theorem. This contrasts with the frequentist approach of point estimates.

§1 Bayes' Theorem §2 Prior & Posterior §3 Conjugate Priors §4 MAP Estimation §5 Bayesian vs Frequentist
01

Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is the foundation of Bayesian learning. It describes how to update the probability of a hypothesis $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ given observed data $\mathcal{D}$.

Equation (1)
Bayes' Theorem
$$ p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) \, p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathcal{D})} $$
Posterior $\propto$ Likelihood $\times$ Prior
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The posterior probability of parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ given data $\mathcal{D}$ equals the likelihood of the data given $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, times the prior probability of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, divided by the marginal likelihood (evidence).”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningRole
$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D})$ Posterior — belief after seeing data Target of inference
$p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta})$ Likelihood — how probable is the data given $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ Connects parameters to data
$p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ Prior — belief before seeing data Encodes domain knowledge
$p(\mathcal{D})$ Evidence / marginal likelihood — normalizing constant $ \int p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \, d\boldsymbol{\theta}$
▶ Intuition
Imagine you have a scale that's slightly biased. Before weighing anything, you suspect the scale reads about right (prior). After weighing a known 1kg weight and getting 1.05kg, you update your belief (posterior) — the scale is biased high by about 5%. Bayes' theorem formalizes this update.
▶ Derivation from Conditional Probability
1
Joint probability: $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathcal{D}) = p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$
product rule
↓
2
Also: $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathcal{D}) = p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) p(\mathcal{D})$
product rule
↓
3
Set equal: $p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) p(\mathcal{D}) = p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$
equate
↓
4
Divide by $p(\mathcal{D})$ to get Bayes' Theorem
$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathcal{D})}$
02

Prior & Posterior Distributions

The prior encodes what we know before seeing data. The posterior combines the prior with the likelihood. As we observe more data, the posterior concentrates around the true parameter.

$p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$

Prior

Subjective belief before data. Can be informative (narrow) or uninformative (wide).

$p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D})$

Posterior

Updated belief after seeing data. Combines prior and likelihood.

$p(\mathcal{D})$

Evidence

Normalizing constant. Used for model comparison (Bayes factors).

▶ Prior → Posterior Update
With more data, the posterior becomes increasingly concentrated (narrower) and less influenced by the prior.
▶ Beta-Bernoulli Example
Setup: Coin with unknown bias $\theta$. Prior: $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(2, 2)$ (peaked at 0.5). Observe 7 heads out of 10 flips.
QuantityFormulaResult
Prior$\text{Beta}(2, 2)$$E[\theta] = 0.5$
Likelihood$\theta^7 (1-\theta)^3$Binomial
Posterior$\text{Beta}(2+7, 2+3) = \text{Beta}(9, 5)$$E[\theta] = 9/14 \approx 0.643$
$\displaystyle \text{Posterior mean: } \frac{9}{14} \approx 0.643$
i
What the posterior mean means

The prior pulled the estimate toward $0.5$, and the data (70% heads) pulled toward $0.7$. The posterior compromise is $0.643$ — a weighted average of prior and likelihood.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Confusing prior with posterior: The prior is before data, posterior after. The posterior becomes the new prior if more data arrives (sequential Bayesian updating).

✖ Ignoring the evidence: $p(\mathcal{D})$ is not always easy to compute. In practice, we often work with $p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ and normalize later.

03

Conjugate Priors

A prior is conjugate to a likelihood if the posterior belongs to the same family as the prior. This makes Bayesian updating analytically tractable, avoiding expensive numerical integration.

Equation (2)
Conjugacy
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Prior: } &\; p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \in \mathcal{F} \\ \text{Likelihood: } &\; p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) \\ \text{Posterior: } &\; p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) \in \mathcal{F} \; \text{(same family)} \end{aligned}$$
If prior and posterior belong to the same distribution family $\mathcal{F}$, the prior is conjugate to the likelihood
▶ Read the Definition in English
“A conjugate prior ensures the posterior has the same form as the prior — just with updated parameters. This turns Bayesian updating into a simple parameter update rule.”
▶ Common Conjugate Pairs
Beta ↔ Bernoulli Prior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$
Likelihood: $\text{Bernoulli}(\theta)$
Posterior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha + \sum x_i, \beta + n - \sum x_i)$
Beta ↔ Binomial Prior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$
Likelihood: $\text{Binomial}(n, \theta)$
Posterior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha + x, \beta + n - x)$
Normal ↔ Normal (known var) Prior: $\N(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$
Likelihood: $\N(\theta, \sigma^2)$
Posterior: $\N(\frac{\mu_0/\sigma_0^2 + \sum x_i / \sigma^2}{1/\sigma_0^2 + n/\sigma^2}, \dots)$
Gamma ↔ Poisson Prior: $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$
Likelihood: $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$
Posterior: $\text{Gamma}(\alpha + \sum x_i, \beta + n)$
▶ Intuition
A conjugate prior is like having a fill-in-the-blank template for the posterior. You just "add" the data counts to the prior parameters. For Beta-Bernoulli, the prior parameters $(\alpha, \beta)$ act as pseudo-counts — $\alpha$ imaginary heads and $\beta$ imaginary tails observed before the experiment.
!
Common Mistake

✖ Assuming every prior is conjugate: Conjugacy is a special property. For complex models (neural networks, GMMs), conjugate priors don't exist — we must use MCMC or variational inference.

04

Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) Estimation

MAP estimation finds the parameter value that maximizes the posterior. It is a compromise between MLE (no prior) and full Bayesian inference (entire posterior). MAP is equivalent to MLE with regularization.

Equation (3)
MAP Estimate
$$ \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; \bigl[ \log p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) + \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \bigr] $$
MAP maximizes the log-posterior = log-likelihood + log-prior (ignoring the constant evidence term)
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The MAP estimate is the parameter that makes the posterior most probable. It's found by maximizing the sum of the log-likelihood and the log-prior — equivalent to MLE plus a penalty from the prior.”
▶ MAP = MLE + Regularization
1
MLE: $\max \log p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta})$
no penalty
↓ Add Gaussian prior $\N(0, \sigma_0^2)$
2
MAP: $\max \bigl[ \log p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) - \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2 \bigr]$
L2 regularization!
↓ Add Laplace prior
3
MAP: $\max \bigl[ \log p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) - \lambda \|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|_1 \bigr]$
L1 regularization!
✓
Key Insight

A Gaussian prior corresponds to L2 (ridge) regularization. A Laplace prior corresponds to L1 (lasso) regularization. This connection bridges Bayesian and frequentist perspectives.

▶ MAP vs MLE Example
Setup: Coin flips: 3 heads out of 10. Prior: $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$. Compare MLE vs MAP.
EstimatorFormulaResult
MLE$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = 3/10$$0.3$
MAP$\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = (3 + 2 - 1) / (10 + 2 + 2 - 2) = 4/12$$\approx 0.333$
Posterior mean$E[\theta \given \mathcal{D}] = (3 + 2) / (10 + 2 + 2) = 5/14$$\approx 0.357$
$\displaystyle \hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}} = 0.3,\quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = 0.333,\quad E[\theta \given \mathcal{D}] = 0.357$
i
Comparing the estimates

MLE (0.3) is purely data-driven. MAP (0.333) is pulled slightly toward 0.5 by the prior. The posterior mean (0.357) is even more conservative — it integrates over the entire posterior rather than just the mode.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Assuming MAP is fully Bayesian: MAP gives a point estimate, not a distribution. It doesn't capture uncertainty — for that, use the full posterior.

✖ Forgetting the prior is part of the model: MAP with a strong prior that conflicts with data can give poor results. Always check prior sensitivity.

05

Bayesian vs Frequentist Comparison

Understanding the philosophical and practical differences between Bayesian and frequentist statistics is essential for exam questions about when to use each approach.

Equation (4)
Philosophical Difference
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Bayesian: } &\quad p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) &&\text{Parameters are random, data are fixed} \\ \text{Frequentist: } &\quad p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) &&\text{Data are random, parameters are fixed (but unknown)} \end{aligned}$$
The fundamental difference: what is treated as random and what is treated as fixed
▶ Head-to-Head Comparison
Parameters
Bayesian: Random variables with distributions
Frequentist: Fixed unknown constants
Uncertainty
Bayesian: Posterior distribution / credible intervals
Frequentist: Confidence intervals (repeated sampling)
Prior
Bayesian: Required (can be uninformative)
Frequentist: Not used
Small data
Bayesian: Works well (prior regularizes)
Frequentist: MLE may overfit
Large data
Bayesian: Posterior concentrates, prior irrelevant
Frequentist: MLE is consistent and efficient
Computation
Bayesian: Often expensive (MCMC / VI)
Frequentist: Usually cheaper (optimization)
▶ Convergence of Bayesian and Frequentist Estimates
As $n \to \infty$, the posterior mean and MLE converge to the same true parameter $\boldsymbol{\theta}^*$ under mild conditions.
i
Credible vs Confidence Intervals

A 95% credible interval (Bayesian): "There is a 95% probability $\theta$ lies in this interval." A 95% confidence interval (frequentist): "If we repeated the experiment many times, 95% of intervals would contain the true $\theta$." They answer different questions!

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Interpreting confidence intervals as credible intervals: A 95% CI does NOT mean "there's 95% probability $\theta$ is in this interval" from the frequentist perspective.

✖ Thinking Bayesian = subjective: Uninformative priors (e.g., Jeffrey's prior) can make Bayesian analysis objective.
