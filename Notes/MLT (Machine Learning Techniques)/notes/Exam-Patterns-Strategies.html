01

Exam Structure & Question Types

The MLT exam tests both conceptual understanding and mathematical derivation. Questions fall into five distinct patterns. Recognizing the pattern is the first step to solving.

▶ Question Pattern Taxonomy
① Formula Application "Given $\mathbf{C}$, find eigenvalues."
Direct computation — know the formula, plug and chug.
② True/False + Justification "Can $[-1, 2]$ be an eigenvector?"
Check conditions systematically.
③ Derivation "Derive the MLE for $\mu$ of a Gaussian."
Follow the step timeline: likelihood → log → differentiate → solve.
④ Conceptual Comparison "Compare K-Means and GMM."
Structure: similarity, difference 1, difference 2, when to use each.
⑤ Missing Value "Total variance $= 25$, two eigenvalues $8, 5$, find the third."
Use trace property: $\sum \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C})$.
▶ Strategy
Before solving, ask: What pattern is this? Each pattern has a specific workflow. Formula application requires accuracy. True/False requires systematic checking. Derivations require structured steps.
⚠
Watch for multi-part questions

Many questions have parts (a), (b), (c). Part (a) is usually setup for (b) and (c). If you're stuck on (a), you can still attempt (b) and (c) using the given answer — partial credit matters!

02

Essential Formula Sheet

These formulas appear in nearly every exam. Master them cold — you should be able to write any of them from memory.

Equation (1)
Covariance Matrix
$$ \mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X} $$
Always center $\mathbf{X}$ first. $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C}) = \sum \lambda_i$.
Equation (2)
Explained Variance Ratio
$$ \frac{\lambda_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i} $$
Cumulative: sum top $k$ ratios until threshold (e.g., $0.95$).
Equation (3)
PCA Projection
$$ \mathbf{z} = \mathbf{W}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x}, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{W} \mathbf{z} $$
$\mathbf{W}$ = top $k$ eigenvectors. Reconstruction error $\|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}\|^2$ is minimized.
Equation (4)
Kernel Definition
$$ K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \phi(\mathbf{x}_i)^\mathsf{T} \phi(\mathbf{x}_j) $$
RBF: $\exp(-\gamma \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|^2)$. Polynomial: $(\mathbf{x}_i^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x}_j + c)^p$.
Equation (5)
K-Means Objective
$$ J = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{k=1}^{K} r_{ik} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k\|^2 $$
Assignment: nearest centroid. Update: $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{n_k} \sum_{i: r_{ik}=1} \mathbf{x}_i$.
Equation (6)
Bayes' Theorem
$$ p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \given \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \given \boldsymbol{\theta}) \, p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathcal{D})} $$
Posterior $\propto$ Likelihood $\times$ Prior. MAP = MLE + log-prior.
i
Memory Aid

For PCA: "Center, Cov, Eig, Sort, Select, Project." Six steps, six seconds to recall.

Each question type follows a repeatable workflow. Memorize these workflows and apply them methodically during the exam.

▶ Workflow A: PCA Question
1
Is data centered? If not, center it.
$\mathbf{X} \leftarrow \mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu}$
↓
2

$\lambda_1 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_d$

$\|\mathbf{v}\| = 1$
↓

Sort by $\lambda$, select top $k$, project
$\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{W}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x}$

04

Common Exam Traps

Every exam has recurring traps that trip up students. Here are the most common ones and how to avoid them.

Equation (7)
The Dimensionality Trap
$$ \underbrace{\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}}_{d \times d} \quad \text{vs} \quad \underbrace{\mathbf{X} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T}}_{n \times n} $$
Which one is the covariance matrix? $\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$ (assuming centered). The other is the Gram matrix.
!
Trap 1: Forgetting to center data

The covariance matrix formula $\frac{1}{n} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$ only works for centered data. If you compute it on raw data, the first PC captures the mean, not the variance.

!
Trap 2: Confusing eigenvectors with PCs

Eigenvectors must be unit length and orthogonal. A candidate vector may satisfy $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}$ but fail the unit-length check. Always verify all three conditions.

!
Trap 3: Kernel PSD check

A symmetric function is not automatically a kernel. You must verify positive semi-definiteness for all possible sets of points, not just one example.

!
Trap 4: K-Means $K$ choice

The elbow is not always clear. If no sharp bend exists, don't force one — use silhouette score, Gap statistic, or domain knowledge instead.

!
Trap 5: Bayesian vs Frequentist interpretation

A 95% confidence interval does not mean "95% probability the parameter is in this interval." That's a credible interval. CIs are about repeated sampling.

▶ Trap Example: Missing Eigenvalue
Problem: Covariance matrix has eigenvalues $[10, ?, 4]$. Total variance is $25$. Find the missing eigenvalue and explained variance of PC1.
StepCalculationResult
1Total variance = sum of eigenvalues$10 + \lambda_2 + 4 = 25$
2Solve: $\lambda_2 = 25 - 14$$11$
3Largest eigenvalue is $11$ (not $10$!)PC1 explains $11/25 = 44\%$
$\displaystyle \lambda_2 = 11,\ \text{PC1 variance ratio} = \frac{11}{25} = 0.44$
i
The hidden trap

Students often assume $\lambda_1$ is the first given number ($10$). But the missing value $11$ is larger, making it $\lambda_1$. Always sort eigenvalues descending before computing ratios.

05

Time Management Strategy

The MLT exam is time-pressured. A strategic approach maximizes your score by allocating time proportionally to question weight and difficulty.

▶ Time Allocation Framework
① Scan (5 min) Read all questions. Identify easy wins and hard problems. Mark confidence levels.
② Easy Wins (30%) Solve formula-application and true/false questions first. They're quick and build momentum.
③ Derivations (40%) Tackle derivations next. Follow the step timeline — partial credit is awarded for correct steps even if the final answer is wrong.
④ Hard Problems (25%) Conceptual comparisons and multi-part questions. If stuck, write relevant formulas and definitions for partial credit.
⑤ Review (5%) Check for missing units, unsorted eigenvalues, uncentered data, sign errors.
▶ Exam Flow Diagram
Flow from easiest to hardest. Reserve 5% at the end for review.
i
Partial Credit Strategy

For derivations, show every step. Even if your final answer is wrong, each correct intermediate step earns marks. Write down the starting equation, the log transform, the derivative — everything.
