K-Means Clustering

K-Means partitions $n$ observations into $K$ clusters by minimizing within-cluster variance. It is the simplest and most widely used unsupervised learning algorithm.

§1 Objective §2 Algorithm §3 Convergence §4 Choosing K §5 Limitations
01

The Objective Function

K-Means minimizes the sum of squared Euclidean distances between each point and its assigned cluster centroid. This is also called the inertia or within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS).

Equation (1)
K-Means Objective
$$ J = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{k=1}^{K} r_{ik} \, \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k\|^2 $$
$r_{ik} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates if point $i$ belongs to cluster $k$. Each point assigned to exactly one cluster: $\sum_k r_{ik} = 1$.
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The K-Means objective $J$ sums over every point $\mathbf{x}_i$ and every cluster $k$: if point $i$ belongs to cluster $k$ ($r_{ik} = 1$), add the squared distance from $\mathbf{x}_i$ to that cluster's centroid $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$. Minimizing $J$ makes points within each cluster as tight as possible.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$J$ Objective (inertia / WCSS) Total squared distance
$r_{ik}$ Assignment indicator (1 if $i$ in cluster $k$, else 0) $r_{1,2} = 1$ means point 1 is in cluster 2
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ Centroid of cluster $k$ (mean of assigned points) $\boldsymbol{\mu}_2 = [3.2, 4.1]$
$\|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k\|^2$ Squared Euclidean distance $(x_{i1} - \mu_{k1})^2 + (x_{i2} - \mu_{k2})^2$
▶ Intuition
Imagine you have to organize a pile of magnets into $K$ groups. You place $K$ magnets as anchors, and each nearby magnet sticks to its closest anchor. Then you move each anchor to the center of the magnets stuck to it, and repeat. The total "stretch" of all magnets is $J$.
$\mathbf{x}_i$

Data Points

The $n$ observations to be clustered, each in $\R^d$.

$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$

Centroids

The $K$ cluster centers, recomputed each iteration.

$r_{ik}$

Assignments

Binary matrix: $1$ if point $i$ in cluster $k$, else $0$.

02

The K-Means Algorithm

K-Means alternates between two steps — assignment and update — until convergence. This is a coordinate descent on $J$.

Equation (2)
Assignment Step (E-step)
$$ r_{ik} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } k = \arg\min_j \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$
Each point is assigned to the nearest centroid
Equation (3)
Update Step (M-step)
$$ \boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{n_k} \sum_{i: r_{ik} = 1} \mathbf{x}_i \quad \text{where } n_k = \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_{ik} $$
Each centroid moves to the mean of its assigned points
▶ Read the Steps in English
“In the assignment step, every point finds the closest centroid and joins that cluster. In the update step, each centroid becomes the arithmetic mean of all points assigned to it. Repeat until centroids stop moving.”

What $J = 2$ means

The total within-cluster squared distance is $2$. Since the clusters are well-separated, $J$ is small. In practice, plotting $J$ vs $K$ (the elbow method) helps choose $K$.

03

Convergence Properties

K-Means is guaranteed to converge to a local minimum of $J$ in a finite number of steps. However, it may not find the global optimum, and different initializations lead to different results.

Equation (4)
Monotonic Decrease
$$ J^{(t+1)} \le J^{(t)} $$
The objective never increases — both steps are guaranteed to reduce or maintain $J$
▶ Why Convergence is Guaranteed
“Each assignment step reduces $J$ by choosing the nearest centroid. Each update step reduces $J$ because the mean minimizes squared distances. Since $J$ is bounded below by $0$ and only $K^n$ possible assignments exist, the algorithm must converge.”
▶ Convergence Behavior
K-Means always converges, but the quality depends on initialization. K-Means++ initialization mitigates this.
⚠
Local vs Global Optimum

K-Means is non-convex. It finds a local minimum, which may be far from the global minimum. Run multiple restarts with different seeds and pick the one with smallest $J$.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Assuming K-Means finds the global optimum: It only guarantees a local minimum. Different initializations yield different results.

✖ Thinking convergence means correct clustering: Convergence means centroids stopped moving, not that the clusters are meaningful or match ground truth.

04

Choosing the Number of Clusters $K$

$K$ is a hyperparameter — we must choose it before running K-Means. The elbow method and silhouette score are the most common approaches.

Equation (5)
Elbow Criterion
$$ \text{Choose } K \text{ where } J(K) \text{ stops decreasing sharply} $$
Plot $J$ (inertia) vs $K$. Look for the "elbow" — the point where adding more clusters gives diminishing returns
Equation (6)
Silhouette Score
$$ s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max\{a(i), b(i)\}} $$
$a(i)$ = mean distance to points in same cluster; $b(i)$ = mean distance to points in nearest other cluster. $s(i) \in [-1, 1]$; higher is better.
▶ Read the Equations in English
“The elbow method picks $K$ where the inertia plot bends. The silhouette score for point $i$ compares how close $i$ is to its own cluster vs the nearest other cluster — a score near $1$ means well-clustered, near $-1$ means misclassified.”

Interpreting the elbow

From $K=1$ to $K=4$, each new cluster dramatically reduces $J$. After $K=4$, improvements are small — we're likely overfitting noise. Choose $K=4$.

i
Exam Tip

The elbow is not always clear. If no sharp bend exists, consider domain knowledge, the silhouette score, or the Gap statistic instead.

05

Limitations & Alternatives

Despite its popularity, K-Means has well-known weaknesses. Understanding them is crucial for exam questions about when not to use it.

▶ Limitations
Assumes spherical clusters K-Means with Euclidean distance assumes clusters are isotropic (spherical). Elongated or irregular clusters are poorly captured.
Sensitive to initialization Different random starts give different results. Mitigate with K-Means++ or multiple restarts.
Requires $K$ in advance Unlike DBSCAN or hierarchical clustering, you must specify $K$ before seeing the data.
Sensitive to outliers A distant outlier can pull a centroid away from the true cluster center.
Hard assignments only Each point belongs to exactly one cluster (no uncertainty). Use Gaussian Mixture Models for soft assignments.
Curse of dimensionality In high dimensions, Euclidean distances become less meaningful. Consider dimensionality reduction first.
▶ When K-Means Fails
K-Means imposes spherical Voronoi partitions. For non-convex shapes, kernel or spectral methods are better.
!
Common Mistakes

✖ Using K-Means on categorical data: K-Means uses Euclidean distance, which is meaningless for categorical features. Use K-Modes instead.

✖ Not standardizing features: Features with larger scales dominate the distance. Always standardize before K-Means.

✖ Assuming $K$ equals number of classes: $K$ is a clustering choice — it may not match the number of ground-truth classes.
