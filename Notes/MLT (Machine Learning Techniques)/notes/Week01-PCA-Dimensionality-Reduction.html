PCA & Dimensionality Reduction

Principal Component Analysis transforms high-dimensional data into a lower-dimensional space while preserving maximum variance. Master the covariance matrix, eigenvalues, eigenvectors, projection, and the dual formulation.

§1 Covariance Matrix §2 Eigenvalues §3 Eigenvectors & PCs §4 Projection §5 Dual PCA
01

The Covariance Matrix

The covariance matrix captures how every pair of features varies together. It is the foundation of PCA — all subsequent analysis flows from its eigenvectors and eigenvalues.

Equation (1)
Covariance Matrix
$$ \mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X} $$
For a centered data matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \R^{n \times d}$, the $d \times d$ covariance matrix measures pairwise feature covariances
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$ equals one over $n$ times the data matrix $\mathbf{X}$ transposed multiplied by $\mathbf{X}$ itself. Each entry $\mathbf{C}_{ij}$ is the covariance between feature $i$ and feature $j$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{X}$ Data matrix, $n$ samples $\times$ $d$ features $100 \times 5$ matrix
$n$ Number of samples (rows) $n = 100$
$\mathbf{C}$ Covariance matrix, $d \times d$, symmetric PSD $\mathbf{C} \in \R^{d \times d}$
$\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T}$ Transpose of $\mathbf{X}$ $d \times n$ after transpose
▶ Intuition
Imagine you have a spreadsheet of exam scores: columns are subjects (Math, Physics, English), rows are students. The covariance matrix tells you which subjects move together — if a student scores high in Math, do they also score high in Physics? If yes, those two features are redundant, and PCA can combine them into one component.
$\mathbf{C}_{ii} = \sigma_i^2$

Diagonal = Variance

Each diagonal entry is the variance of feature $i$ alone.

$\mathbf{C}_{ij} = \mathbf{C}_{ji}$

Symmetric

Covariance between $i$ and $j$ is the same as $j$ and $i$.

$\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{C}) = \sum \lambda_i$

Trace = Total Variance

The sum of diagonal entries equals the total variance in the data.

▶ Numerical Example
Setup: Two features, $n = 3$ centered points: $\mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 \\ -1 & 1 \\ 0 & -3\end{pmatrix}$
StepOperationResult
1 Compute $\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$ $\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 1 & 14\end{pmatrix}$
2 Divide by $n = 3$ $\mathbf{C} = \begin{pmatrix}0.667 & 0.333 \\ 0.333 & 4.667\end{pmatrix}$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{C} = \begin{pmatrix}0.667 & 0.333 \\ 0.333 & 4.667\end{pmatrix}$
i
What these values mean

The variance of feature 1 is $0.667$, feature 2 is $4.667$ — so feature 2 has much more spread. The covariance $0.333$ is positive, meaning the two features tend to increase together.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Forgetting to center data: PCA always assumes mean-centered data. If you forget, the first PC captures the mean, not the variance.

✖ Confusing covariance with correlation: Covariance is unscaled and depends on units. Correlation is scaled to $[-1, 1]$.

02

Eigenvalues & Explained Variance

Eigenvalues tell us how much variance each principal component captures. The ratio $\lambda_k / \sum \lambda_i$ is the fraction of total variance explained by the $k$-th PC — the central metric for deciding how many components to keep.

Equation (2)
Characteristic Equation
$$ \det(\mathbf{C} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0 $$
Solve this polynomial to find all $d$ eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_d \ge 0$
Equation (3)
Explained Variance Ratio
$$ \text{Explained variance by PC}_k = \frac{\lambda_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i} $$
The proportion of total variance captured by the $k$-th principal component
▶ Read the Equations in English
“Eigenvalues are the roots of $\det(\mathbf{C} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0$. Each $\lambda_k$ measures the variance along the $k$-th PC direction. Dividing by the sum gives the fraction of total variance explained.”
▶ Intuition
Imagine a shoebox filled with points. The longest dimension of the box (PC1) has the largest spread — that's $\lambda_1$. The next longest perpendicular dimension (PC2) has spread $\lambda_2$, and so on. The sum of all spreads is the total variance. If $\lambda_1$ accounts for $80\%$ of the total, then PC1 captures most of the information.
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: A $3 \times 3$ covariance matrix has eigenvalues $\lambda = [10, 6, 4]$.
PCEigenvalueExplained VarianceCumulative
PC1$10$$10/20 = 50\%$$50\%$
PC2$6$$6/20 = 30\%$$80\%$
PC3$4$$4/20 = 20\%$$100\%$
$\displaystyle \text{Total variance} = 10 + 6 + 4 = 20$
i
Exam Trick

When one eigenvalue is missing and total variance is given, compute: $\lambda_{\text{missing}} = \text{total variance} - \sum(\text{known})$. Then compute explained variance ratios directly.

i
Interpreting the ratios

If the first two PCs explain $80\%$ of variance, reducing from 3D to 2D retains $80\%$ of the information. The remaining $20\%$ is noise or fine detail — dropping it simplifies analysis with minimal loss.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Assuming eigenvalues are the data values: Eigenvalues are variances along PCs, not the data themselves.

✖ Using raw eigenvalues: Always normalize by the sum to get proportions — a $\lambda$ of 50 could mean everything or nothing depending on total variance.

03

Eigenvectors & Principal Components

Each eigenvector of the covariance matrix defines a principal component direction. To be a valid PC, a vector must satisfy three conditions simultaneously.

Equation (4)
Eigenvector Conditions
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbf{C} \mathbf{v} &= \lambda \mathbf{v} &&\text{(eigenvector equation)} \\ \|\mathbf{v}\| &= 1 &&\text{(unit length)} \\ \mathbf{v}_i \cdot \mathbf{v}_j &= 0 \;(i \ne j) &&\text{(orthogonal)} \end{aligned}$$
Three checks every valid principal component must pass
▶ Read the Condition in English
“A vector $\mathbf{v}$ is a principal component if (1) multiplying by $\mathbf{C}$ just scales it by $\lambda$, (2) its length is exactly 1, and (3) it is perpendicular to every other PC.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{v}$ Eigenvector / PC direction $\mathbf{v} = [0.6, 0.8]^\mathsf{T}$
$\lambda$ Eigenvalue — variance along $\mathbf{v}$ $\lambda = 10$
$\mathbf{C} \mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}$ Direction is preserved under $\mathbf{C}$ Scales but doesn't rotate
▶ Intuition
Picture a rubber sheet stretched across a frame. The sheet has natural lines where it stretches the most (PC1) and perpendicular to that where it stretches next most (PC2). Eigenvectors are those natural stretching directions. The eigenvalues tell you how much stretch in each direction.
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: $\mathbf{C} = \begin{pmatrix}5 & 1 \\ 1 & 2\end{pmatrix}$. Check if $\mathbf{v} = [1, -2]^\mathsf{T}$ can be a PC.
CheckCalculationVerdict
Unit length?$\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{1 + 4} = \sqrt{5} \ne 1$✖ Fail
Eigenvector?$\mathbf{C}\mathbf{v} = ?$Needs checking
$\displaystyle \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{5} \neq 1 \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{Not a valid PC}$
i
Why unit length matters

Without unit length, the variance along the direction is confounded with the length of the vector. Normalizing ensures $\mathbf{v}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{C} \mathbf{v} = \lambda$ gives the true variance.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Any vector with large variance is a PC: False. It must be an eigenvector of $\mathbf{C}$. High variance alone is not enough.

✖ Checking only one condition: A candidate must pass all three: eigenvector equation, unit length, and orthogonality to previous PCs.

04

PCA Projection

Once we have the top $k$ eigenvectors (PCs), we project each data point onto them to get a lower-dimensional representation. This is how PCA achieves dimensionality reduction.

Equation (5)
Projection
$$ \mathbf{z} = \mathbf{W}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x} $$
$\mathbf{W}$ is the $d \times k$ matrix of top $k$ eigenvectors; $\mathbf{z} \in \R^k$ is the reduced representation
Equation (6)
Reconstruction
$$ \hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{W} \mathbf{z} $$
Reconstructing the original $d$-dimensional point from its $k$-dimensional code — the error $\|\mathbf{x} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}\|^2$ is minimized by PCA
▶ Read the Equations in English
“To reduce a point $\mathbf{x}$ from $d$ dimensions to $k$, multiply the transpose of $\mathbf{W}$ by $\mathbf{x}$ — this gives coordinates $\mathbf{z}$ in the PC space. To reconstruct, multiply $\mathbf{W}$ by $\mathbf{z}$ — PCA guarantees this reconstruction has the smallest possible squared error.”
▶ Step-by-Step Projection
1
Center the data point: subtract the mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$
$\mathbf{x}_{\text{centered}} = \mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}$
↓
2
Stack top $k$ eigenvectors into matrix $\mathbf{W} = [\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_k]$
$\mathbf{W} \in \R^{d \times k}$
↓
3
Project: $\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{W}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x}_{\text{centered}}$
$\mathbf{z} \in \R^k$
↓
4
Reconstruct: $\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{W} \mathbf{z} + \boldsymbol{\mu}$
$\hat{\mathbf{x}} \in \R^d$
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: PC1 direction $\mathbf{v}_1 = [1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2}]^\mathsf{T}$, centered point $\mathbf{x} = [3, 1]$. Reduce to 1D.
StepCalculationResult
1$z = \mathbf{v}_1^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x}$$3/\sqrt{2} + 1/\sqrt{2} = 4/\sqrt{2}$
2Simplify$2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$
$\displaystyle z = \frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$
i
What the projection means

The original point $[3, 1]$ in 2D becomes a single number $2.828$ in 1D. This number is the coordinate along PC1 — the direction of maximum variance. We've reduced from 2D to 1D while preserving as much variance as possible.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Forgetting to center: Projecting uncentered data gives incorrect coordinates.

✖ Confusing $\mathbf{W}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{x}$ with $\mathbf{W} \mathbf{x}$: The projection uses the transpose of $\mathbf{W}$ because $\mathbf{W}$ columns are the PCs and we need dot products.

05

Dual PCA — When $n \ll d$

Standard PCA works with the $d \times d$ covariance matrix $\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$. But when the number of features $d$ far exceeds samples $n$, it's cheaper to work with the $n \times n$ Gram matrix $\mathbf{X} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T}$.

Equation (7)
Gram Matrix
$$ \mathbf{G} = \mathbf{X} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} $$
The $n \times n$ matrix of pairwise dot-products between samples — dual PCA works with this instead of $\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The Gram matrix $\mathbf{G}$ equals $\mathbf{X}$ times its transpose — an $n \times n$ matrix where entry $\mathbf{G}_{ij}$ is the dot product between sample $i$ and sample $j$. Its non-zero eigenvalues are identical to those of the covariance matrix.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{G}$ Gram matrix, $n \times n$ $30 \times 30$ when $n=30$
$\mathbf{X} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T}$ Dot products between samples Measures sample similarity
$\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$ Covariance (feature similarity) $d \times d$, expensive when $d$ large
▶ Intuition
Standard PCA asks “Which features vary together?” (a $d \times d$ question). Dual PCA asks “Which samples are similar to each other?” (an $n \times n$ question). Both give the same eigenvalues, but one is cheaper depending on data shape.
▶ When to Use Dual PCA
Scenario: Genomics data with $n = 50$ patients and $d = 10{,}000$ genes.
MethodMatrix SizeTime Complexity
Standard PCA$10000 \times 10000$$O(d^2 n) = 5 \times 10^9$
Dual PCA$50 \times 50$$O(n^2 d) = 2.5 \times 10^7$
$\displaystyle \text{Dual PCA is } \approx 200\times \text{ faster here}$
▶ Dual vs Standard PCA Decision Flow
Decision rule: if $n < d$, the Gram matrix is smaller — use Dual PCA.
✓
Key Insight

Non-zero eigenvalues of $\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{X} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T}$ are identical. Only the eigenvectors differ — the dual gives eigenvectors of the Gram matrix, which relate to the original via $\mathbf{v} \propto \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{u}$.

▶ Applications of Dual PCA
Genomics $50$ samples, $10000$ genes — Dual PCA makes eigenvalue computation tractable.
Kernel PCA Nonlinear extensions naturally use the Gram (kernel) matrix.
Image Processing Small images ($28 \times 28$) but many pixels ($784$) — $n$ is often smaller than $d$.
Anomaly Detection Projecting onto dual PCs reveals outliers in low-sample regimes.
!
Common Mistakes

✖ Thinking Dual PCA gives different eigenvalues: False. Non-zero eigenvalues of $\mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{X} \mathbf{X}^\mathsf{T}$ are identical.

✖ Using Dual PCA when $d < n$: The $d \times d$ covariance is smaller — standard PCA is more efficient.
