Week 01

Dimensionality Reduction & Linear PCA

The Problem

High-dimensional datasets (e.g., $1000 \times 1000$ pixel images) are hard to visualize, computationally heavy, and prone to overfitting. Many features are highly correlated or redundant. Simple column dropping throws away data. How do we reduce dimensions while preserving maximum information?

Mental Model

Think of a 3D cloud of data. Instead of observing it along the standard $x,y,z$ axes, we want to find a new camera angle (orthogonal coordinate system) where the first axis points along the direction of maximum spread (variance) of the cloud, and the second axis is perpendicular to the first, holding the next highest spread.

Real-Life Intuition: The Shadow Projection

Imagine holding a 3D teapot in front of a flashlight, projecting its shadow onto a 2D wall. If you point the flashlight from the front, the shadow might collapse into a flat circle, hiding the handle and spout. To capture the "true shape" of the teapot in 2D, you must rotate it until the shadow is as wide and detailed as possible. The orientation that produces the shadow with the greatest spread (variance) represents the first principal component directions.

Math in a Nutshell $$ \max_{\mathbf{w}} \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{w} \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{w} = 1 $$ $$ \text{Formulating the Lagrangian: } \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda) = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{w} - \lambda(\mathbf{w}^T\mathbf{w} - 1) $$ $$ \text{Setting derivative to zero gives the Eigenvalue Problem: } \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{w} = \lambda \mathbf{w} $$

$\mathbf{\Sigma}$ is the sample covariance matrix $\frac{1}{N}X^T X$ (for centered $X$). The projection direction $\mathbf{w}$ is an eigenvector of $\mathbf{\Sigma}$, and the corresponding eigenvalue $\lambda$ is the variance captured along that direction.

Week 02

Non-Linear PCA & The Kernel Trick

The Problem

Linear PCA only works if the data lies on a flat, linear subspace. If the dataset forms non-linear shapes (like concentric circles, spirals, or Swiss rolls), linear PCA will compress them by smashing them into each other, completely destroying the grouping boundaries and structure.

Mental Model

Map the input data non-linearly into a much higher-dimensional space where the curved structure "unfolds" and becomes linear. Once unfolded, we can perform standard linear PCA. Crucially, we bypass calculating the coordinates in that massive space by computing only pairwise distances/similarities.

Real-Life Intuition: Lifting the Flat Paper

Imagine a flat piece of paper with a circle of blue dots inside a outer ring of red dots. In 2D, you cannot separate these with a straight line. Now, imagine poking the center of the paper from below, pushing the blue dots up to form a 3D cone. The red dots remain on the table. Now, a flat sheet of cardboard (a 2D plane) can easily slide between the blue dots and red dots to separate them. Higher dimensions simplify non-linear structures.

Math in a Nutshell $$ \text{Feature mapping: } \mathbf{x} \to \Phi(\mathbf{x}) \implies \text{Covariance in feature space: } \mathbf{\Sigma}_\Phi = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \Phi(\mathbf{x}_i)\Phi(\mathbf{x}_i)^T $$ $$ \text{Eigenvector is a linear combination of mapped points: } \mathbf{w} = \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i \Phi(\mathbf{x}_i) $$ $$ \text{Substituting gives the Kernel Eigenvalue Problem: } \widetilde{\mathbf{K}} \boldsymbol{\alpha} = \lambda \boldsymbol{\alpha} $$ $$ \text{Where } \mathbf{K}_{ij} = k(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \langle\Phi(\mathbf{x}_i), \Phi(\mathbf{x}_j)\rangle \quad \text{(Kernel Trick - no explicit } \Phi(\mathbf{x}) \text{ calculation)} $$

$\widetilde{\mathbf{K}}$ is the centered kernel matrix: $\widetilde{\mathbf{K}} = \mathbf{K} - \mathbf{1}_N\mathbf{K} - \mathbf{K}\mathbf{1}_N + \mathbf{1}_N\mathbf{K}\mathbf{1}_N$ where $\mathbf{1}_N$ is a matrix with elements $1/N$.

Week 03

K-Means Clustering & Initialization

The Problem

Given a collection of unlabeled data points (e.g. historical credit card transactions), how do we automatically group them into $K$ distinct clusters such that points within each cluster are highly similar, while separate clusters are distinct?

Mental Model

Lloyd's algorithm uses iterative coordinate descent. You place $K$ pins randomly. Then alternate: 1. Assign: Every data point registers with the nearest pin. 2. Update: Each pin moves to the physical center of gravity (mean) of all its registered points. Repeat until the pins stop moving.

Real-Life Intuition: Franchise Store Placement

Imagine placing $K$ pizza delivery hubs across a city. Initially, you place them randomly. Customers always order from their nearest hub (Assignment). After a month, you look at all orders for Hub A and move Hub A to the geographic center of its actual customers (Update). You do this for all hubs and repeat. Over time, the hubs migrate to the perfect central positions, minimizing the average travel distance for all customers.

Math in a Nutshell $$ \text{Objective: Minimize Distortion (Inertia): } J(\mathbf{r}, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{k=1}^K r_{ik} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k\|^2 $$ $$ \text{E-step (Assignment): } r_{ik} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } k = \arg\min_j \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$ $$ \text{M-step (Update Centroids): } \boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N r_{ik} \mathbf{x}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N r_{ik}} $$

Since both steps minimize $J$, K-Means is guaranteed to converge. However, it can get trapped in local minima. K-Means++ resolves this by initializing centers sequentially: choose the next center with probability proportional to the squared distance to the nearest existing center $D(\mathbf{x})^2$.

Week 04

Parameter Estimation, GMM & EM

The Problem

1. Estimation: How do we determine the parameters of a probability distribution from observed data? 2. Overlapping Clusters: K-Means uses "hard" assignments (0 or 1) and assumes spherical boundaries. If clusters overlap, have elliptical shapes, or vary in density, K-Means fails.

Mental Model

MLE vs MAP: Tune distribution knobs to maximize likelihood of observed data. MLE is purely data-driven; MAP includes a prior belief to regularize.
GMM & EM: Model data as a mixture of $K$ different bell curves. Since we don't know which point came from which bell curve (latent variable), we run the EM algorithm: guess labels softly (E-step), update bell curve shapes based on guesses (M-step).

Real-Life Intuition: Separating Genders by Height

Imagine you have a list of heights of 1000 people, but their gender labels (Male/Female) are hidden. You know height distribution is a mixture of two normal curves (Male and Female).
E-step: Look at a person's height. If they are 6'1", assign a 90% probability of being Male. If 5'2", assign a 15% probability of being Male. These are responsibilities.
M-step: Recalculate the average height of males by computing a weighted average of heights of all 1000 people, using their "Male probability" as the weight. Do the same for females. Repeat.

Math in a Nutshell $$ \text{MLE: } \theta_{\text{MLE}} = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{i=1}^N \ln p(\mathbf{x}_i \given \theta) \quad \Big| \quad \text{MAP: } \theta_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \left[ \sum_{i=1}^N \ln p(\mathbf{x}_i \given \theta) + \ln p(\theta) \right] $$ $$ \text{GMM Formulation: } p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \given \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k) \quad \text{where } \sum_k \pi_k = 1 $$ $$ \text{EM E-Step (Responsibilities): } \gamma_{ik} = \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \given \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^K \pi_j \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \given \boldsymbol{\mu}_j, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)} $$ $$ \text{EM M-Step Updates: } \boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{\sum_i \gamma_{ik}\mathbf{x}_i}{N_k}, \quad \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k = \frac{\sum_i \gamma_{ik}(\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)(\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^T}{N_k}, \quad \pi_k = \frac{N_k}{N} \quad \text{with } N_k = \sum_i \gamma_{ik} $$ $$ \text{Jensen's Lower Bound (ELBO): } \ln p(X \given \theta) = \ln \sum_Z q(Z) \frac{p(X, Z \given \theta)}{q(Z)} \ge \sum_Z q(Z) \ln \frac{p(X, Z \given \theta)}{q(Z)} $$

Jensen's Inequality states that for a concave function $f$, $f(\mathbb{E}[X]) \ge \mathbb{E}[f(X)]$. The logarithm is concave, allowing EM to maximize the ELBO lower bound, which guarantees the true log-likelihood never decreases.

Core Comparisons

Aspect K-Means Clustering Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM)
Assignment Type Hard assignment (0 or 1) Soft assignment (probabilities / responsibilities $\gamma_{ik}$)
Cluster Boundary Shape Spherical / Linear Voronoi Cells (Euclidean) Elliptical / Flexible (Covariance matrices $\mathbf{\Sigma}_k$)
Underlying Model Geometric / Distance-based heuristic Probabilistic / Generative density estimator
Aspect Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) Maximum A Posteriori (MAP)
Philosophy Frequentist: Parameters are fixed, unknown constants. Bayesian: Parameters are random variables with a prior.
Objective Maximize $p(X \given \theta)$ (likelihood of the data) Maximize $p(X \given \theta)p(\theta)$ (posterior probability)
Overfitting Risk High, especially with small sample sizes Low, prior acts as a regularizer (e.g. L2 penalty)
