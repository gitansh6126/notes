Estimation:
from data to the machine that made it

Every dataset we've clustered or projected so far was treated as a fixed object. This lecture asks a different question: what if the data was generated — and what if we could work backward to the mechanism that produced it?

§1 Paradigm Shift §2 Probabilistic Model §3 Biased Coin §4 IID Assumptions §5 What Is Next
01

A paradigm shift

Unsupervised learning has historically leaned on two deterministic paradigms. Estimation introduces something new: a probabilistic lens — data without labels, but with a hidden mechanism that generated it.

$\text{PCA}$

Representation Learning

PCA and Kernel PCA uncover linear or non-linear structure — compressing data into a lower-dimensional shape.

$K\text{-Means}$

Clustering

$K$-Means groups points by proximity, with no notion of "how sure" a grouping is.

$\boldsymbol{\theta}$

Estimation — Probabilistic

Assumes a hidden mechanism generated the data, and asks: what parameters most plausibly produced what we observed?

§ Paradigm Shift Flow
The old lens sees fixed data points; the new lens sees random outcomes generated by an unknown mechanism.
02

The probabilistic model

Instead of treating a dataset as static, we imagine a probabilistic mechanism — governed by some unknown parameter $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ — that generated every observation. Estimation runs the arrow backward: from data to parameter.

Equation (1)
Data-Generating Distribution
$$\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N \sim p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$$
The observed data is assumed to be drawn from a distribution parameterized by $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“Each observation $\mathbf{x}_i$ is a random sample drawn from a probability distribution $p$ that depends on an unknown parameter $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. The goal of estimation is to find the $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ that best explains the data we actually saw.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{x}_i$ The $i$-th observed data point $\mathbf{x}_1 = 172 \text{ cm}$
$p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$ Probability distribution of the data, given parameters $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ or $\N(\mu, \sigma^2)$
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ Unknown parameters that define the distribution $\theta = p$ for coin flips
▶ Intuition
Imagine a black box that produces numbers. You can't see inside — but you see the output. Your job is to guess what mechanism is inside just by looking at what comes out. If it produces mostly 1s and some 0s, you'd guess a biased coin. If it produces bell-shaped real numbers, you'd guess a Gaussian.

Once $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is estimated, it becomes a compressed summary of the entire dataset — enough to explain the mechanism and even generate new, similar data. A dataset of a million coin flips can be summarized by a single number $p$.
▶ Estimation Flow
The forward arrow (generation) runs from $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ to data. The backward arrow (estimation) infers $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ from data.
i
Why this matters

A dataset of a million coin flips can be summarized by a single number $p$. That's the appeal of estimation: enormous compression, with an explanation attached.

03

Conceptual example — the biased coin

Picture a black box holding a coin with unknown bias $p$ — the probability of landing heads. Press the button, and it flips the coin. The only thing you ever see is the outcome.

Equation (2)
Coin Flip Model (Bernoulli)
$$x_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad P(x_i = 1) = p, \quad P(x_i = 0) = 1-p$$
Each flip is a Bernoulli trial with unknown success probability $p$
▶ Read the Model in English
“Each coin flip $x_i$ is either $1$ (heads) with probability $p$, or $0$ (tails) with probability $1-p$. The flips are independent — knowing one tells you nothing about the next. The goal is to estimate $p$ from the observed sequence of heads and tails.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$x_i$ Outcome of the $i$-th flip ($1$ = heads, $0$ = tails) $x_1 = 1, x_2 = 0, x_3 = 1$
$p$ Unknown probability of heads (the bias) $p = 0.75$
$\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ A distribution over $\{0, 1\}$ with mean $p$ $p = 0.5 \implies$ fair coin
▶ Intuition
If you flip a coin 12 times and see 9 heads, a natural estimate is $\hat{p} = 9/12 = 0.75$. You can also rule out impossible values — $p = 0$ or $p = 1$ — the moment you've seen both outcomes.

But you can never be 100% certain of the true $p$. Estimation gives us the most plausible value, and a way to compare candidates — not certainty.
▶ Step-by-Step: From Data to Estimate
1
Observe a sequence of $N$ coin flips
$\mathbf{x} = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_N\}$
↓
2
Count how many are heads (1s)
$n_h = \sum_{i=1}^N x_i$
↓
3
The fraction of heads is a natural estimate of $p$
$\hat{p} = \frac{n_h}{N}$
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: 12 flips, 9 heads.
MetricValueCalculation
$N$ $12$ Total number of flips
$n_h$ $9$ Total heads
$\hat{p}$ $0.75$ $9 / 12 = 0.75$
$\displaystyle \hat{p} = \frac{9}{12} = 0.75$ — the intuitive estimate matches the data perfectly
i
What this means

An estimate of $\hat{p} = 0.75$ means that, under our model, a heads is 3 times more likely than a tails. But this is just one plausible value — other values near $0.75$ are also reasonably consistent with the data. The art of estimation is quantifying both the best guess and the uncertainty around it.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Certainty illusion: After 9 heads in 12 flips, we cannot be 100% sure $p = 0.75$. The true $p$ could be $0.7$ or $0.8$ — we just saw strong evidence for $0.75$.

✖ Ignoring sample size: $\hat{p} = 3/4 = 0.75$ from 4 flips is far less reliable than $\hat{p} = 75/100 = 0.75$ from 100 flips, even though the fraction is identical.

04

Foundational assumptions — i.i.d.

To formalize estimation over observations $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N$, we lean on two assumptions — together known as i.i.d.: independent and identically distributed.

Independence

$P(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \mathbf{x}_j) = P(\mathbf{x}_i)$ for all $i \neq j$. One observation tells you nothing about the next — no memory, no dependence.

Identically Distributed

Every observation comes from the same distribution: $P(\mathbf{x}_i = 1) = P(\mathbf{x}_j = 1) = p$ for all $i, j$.

Equation (3)
Joint Probability Under IID
$$P(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} P(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$$
The joint probability factorizes into a product of identical per-point terms
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The probability of seeing the entire dataset together is simply the product of each individual point's probability. This is only valid if (a) each point comes from the same distribution and (b) knowing one point doesn't change the probability of another.”
▶ IID Breakdown
AssumptionEveryday AnalogyFormal Statement
Independence A coin has no memory — it doesn't "owe" you a tails after three heads in a row $P(x_i \mid x_j) = P(x_i)$
Identically Distributed You're always drawing from the same vending machine, never a swapped-out one $P(x_i = 1) = P(x_j = 1)$
▶ Intuition
Independence alone would allow the coin's bias to drift between flips. Identical distribution alone would allow flips to be correlated. Both together let us treat every observation as an equally weighted, unbiased clue about the same $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

Formally, i.i.d. means the joint likelihood factorizes: $P(\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N) = \prod P(\mathbf{x}_i)$. This product structure is what makes calculus-based optimization possible — at least until we get to mixtures, where a sum gets trapped inside the product.
▶ Why Both Assumptions Are Needed
1
With only independence, each flip could have a different bias
$p_i$ could differ per $i$
↓
2
With only identical distribution, flips could be correlated
$P(x_i \mid x_j) \neq P(x_i)$ possible
↓
3
With both, the joint probability becomes a simple product
$\displaystyle P(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_i P(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$
i
Why both together

Estimation needs both to treat every observation as an equally weighted, unbiased clue about the same $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. The product structure enables MLE and Bayesian inference.

05

Where this leaves us

The intuitive estimator — just take the fraction of heads — already gives a reasonable answer. But "reasonable" isn't a proof.

naive_estimator.py — the intuitive (but unjustified) approach
# count heads as 1, tails as 0
def estimate_bias(flips):
    heads = sum(flips)
    n = len(flips)
    return heads / n  # p̂ = 9/12 = 0.75

# works, but: why is this THE right estimator,
# and not some other function of the data?
Equation (4)
The Fundamental Question
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} P(\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$$
Maximum Likelihood Estimation: find the $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ that makes the observed data most probable
