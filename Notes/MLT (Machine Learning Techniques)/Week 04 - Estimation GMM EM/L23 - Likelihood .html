Likelihood of GMMs:
when the log stops helping

Logs are supposed to turn hard products into easy sums. For a Gaussian Mixture Model, that trick jams — the mixture puts a sum inside the log, and the algebra simply won't come apart.

01

Recap — the three unknowns

A GMM assumes every point comes from one of K Gaussians. Fitting it means estimating three families of parameters, together written θ.

μ₁ … μ_K

Means

The center of each component's cluster.

σ₁² … σ_K²

Variances

How spread out each cluster is.

π₁ … π_K

Mixing proportions

How likely each component is to be picked. Σ πₖ = 1.

02

The likelihood function

For a single point, we don't know which component generated it — so its density is a weighted average over all K possibilities. That's the marginal density.

marginal density of one point
p(x_i | θ) = Σₖ πₖ · 𝒩(x_i | μₖ, σₖ²)
weighted sum across all K components — this is where the trouble starts
full likelihood, over N i.i.d. points
L(θ) = ∏ᵢ [ Σₖ πₖ · 𝒩(x_i | μₖ, σₖ²) ]
a product over data points (from i.i.d.) — of sums over components
i
Same recipe as before

Because observations are i.i.d., the joint likelihood is still a clean product across data points — that part hasn't changed since the single-Gaussian case.

03

The log-sum bottleneck

Taking the log usually turns a product of terms into a sum of logs — easy to differentiate. Try it on both cases below and watch what happens to the sum sitting inside each mixture term.

// try to push the log inside
K = 1 · single Gaussian
log ∏ᵢ 𝒩(x_i | μ, σ²)
 
✓ becomes Σᵢ log 𝒩(x_i|μ,σ²) — clean, solvable
K > 1 · mixture
log ∏ᵢ [ Σₖ πₖ 𝒩(x_i|μₖ,σₖ²) ]
 
✗ stuck at Σᵢ log( Σₖ … ) — log can't enter the inner sum
!
Why this blocks everything downstream

log(a + b) ≠ log a + log b. The mixture's inner sum survives the log untouched, so the neat term-by-term derivative that worked for a single Gaussian no longer isolates any single μₖ, σₖ², or πₖ.

04

Why MLE stalls here

Two compounding difficulties keep the GMM log-likelihood from being solved the way we solved a single Gaussian.

No closed-form solution

Setting ∂ log L / ∂θ = 0 no longer isolates a clean formula (like the sample mean). Each parameter's equation stays entangled with every other component through the trapped inner sum.

Constrained optimization

The mixing proportions must satisfy Σ πₖ = 1 and πₖ ≥ 0. Any optimizer has to respect that constraint on every step, not just find an unconstrained maximum.

05

Where this leaves us

Generic optimizers like gradient ascent can grind through this numerically — but they ignore the fact that the model has a clean latent structure (component → sample) sitting right behind the messy formula.
