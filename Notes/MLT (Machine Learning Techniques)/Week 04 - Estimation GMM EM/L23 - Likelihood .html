Unsupervised Learning · Lecture Notes

Likelihood of GMMs:
when the log stops helping

Logs are supposed to turn hard products into easy sums. For a Gaussian Mixture Model, that trick jams — the mixture puts a sum inside the log, and the algebra simply won't come apart.

§1 Recap §2 Marginal Density §3 Full Likelihood §4 Log-Likelihood §5 Why MLE Stalls
01

Recap — the three unknowns

A GMM assumes every point comes from one of $K$ Gaussians. Fitting it means estimating three families of parameters, together written $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

$\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 \dots \boldsymbol{\mu}_K$

Means

The center of each component's cluster.

$\sigma_1^2 \dots \sigma_K^2$

Variances

How spread out each cluster is.

$\pi_1 \dots \pi_K$

Mixing proportions

How likely each component is to be picked. $\sum \pi_k = 1$.

§ Recap Flow
The generative story: roll a $K$-sided die, sample from the chosen Gaussian, hide the label $z$.
02

The marginal density of one point

For a single point, we don't know which component generated it — so its density is a weighted average over all $K$ possibilities. This is the marginal density — the core equation of every GMM.

Equation (1)
Marginal Density
$$p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2)$$
The probability of one point is the weighted sum across every Gaussian component
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The probability of observing data point $\mathbf{x}_i$, given the parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, equals the sum over $k$ from $1$ to $K$ of the mixing weight $\pi_k$ multiplied by the Gaussian density of $\mathbf{x}_i$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ and variance $\sigma_k^2$.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{x}_i$ A single data point (the $i$-th observation) $\text{Height} = 172\text{ cm}$
$\boldsymbol{\theta}$ All model parameters $\{\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2, \pi_k\}_{k=1}^K$
$\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{K}$ Sum over all $K$ components (add, don't multiply) $K = 3 \implies 3$ weighted terms added
$\pi_k$ Mixing coefficient — probability of picking component $k$ $\pi_1 = 0.5$
$\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \sigma^2)$ Gaussian (normal) density — bell curve height at point $\mathbf{x}$ $\mathcal{N}(172 \mid 170, 25) \approx 0.079$
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ Mean of the $k$-th component — its center $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 = 170$
$\sigma_k^2$ Variance of the $k$-th component — its spread $\sigma_1^2 = 25$
▶ Intuition
Imagine you hear a sound from behind a wall. There are three speakers ($K = 3$). Each speaker plays a different note, but you can't see which speaker is active. The sound you hear is a weighted blend of all three — some speakers are louder (higher $\pi$) than others.

The marginal density does exactly that: instead of picking one Gaussian, it considers every possible component, weights each by its probability $\pi_k$, and adds them together. The result is a single number saying how likely that sound (data point) is under the overall model.
▶ Step-by-Step Construction
1
Pick a component $k$ with probability $\pi_k$
$k \sim \text{Categorical}(\boldsymbol{\pi})$
↓
2
Compute the Gaussian density for that component at point $\mathbf{x}_i$
$\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2)$
↓
3
Multiply by the mixing weight $\pi_k$
$\pi_k \cdot \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2)$
↓
4
Repeat for every component $k = 1, 2, \dots, K$
$K$ separate weighted densities
↓
5
Add all $K$ weighted densities together
$\displaystyle p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2)$
▶ Visual Flow: Weighted Sum of Gaussians
Each component contributes a weighted density. The final result is the sum of all three contributions.
▶ Numerical Example ($K = 2$, 1-dimensional)
Setup: A GMM with two components. Given point $\mathbf{x} = 172$, compute its marginal density.
$k$$\pi_k$$\mu_k$$\sigma_k^2$$\mathcal{N}(172 \mid \mu_k, \sigma_k^2)$$\pi_k \cdot \mathcal{N}_k$
1 $0.6$ $170$ $25$ $\displaystyle\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \cdot 25}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(172-170)^2}{2 \cdot 25}\right) = 0.0798 \cdot 0.9231 = \mathbf{0.0737}$ $0.6 \cdot 0.0737 = \mathbf{0.0442}$
2 $0.4$ $165$ $36$ $\displaystyle\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \cdot 36}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(172-165)^2}{2 \cdot 36}\right) = 0.0665 \cdot 0.5061 = \mathbf{0.0337}$ $0.4 \cdot 0.0337 = \mathbf{0.0135}$
$\displaystyle p(\mathbf{x} = 172 \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = 0.0442 + 0.0135 = \mathbf{0.0577}$
i
What this value means

A density of 0.0577 means that near $\mathbf{x} = 172$, the model expects about 5.8% of the probability mass per unit of $\mathbf{x}$. If the density were larger (say $0.15$), the point would be more plausible under this model. If smaller (say $0.001$), it would be in a low-density region — possibly between clusters or far from all means.

Changing each variable: Increasing $\pi_1$ to $0.8$ raises the final density to $0.0737 \cdot 0.8 + 0.0135 = \mathbf{0.0725}$. Moving $\mu_1$ from $170$ to $172$ raises the first component's density to its maximum $(0.0798)$, giving $0.0798 \cdot 0.6 + 0.0135 = \mathbf{0.0614}$.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Multiplying instead of adding: A GMM is a sum of weighted Gaussians, not a product. $p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum \pi_k \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x})$, not $\prod \pi_k \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x})$.

✖ Forgetting that the weights sum to 1: $\sum \pi_k = 1$. If you use $\pi_1 = 0.7$ and $\pi_2 = 0.7$, the density is no longer a valid probability distribution.

✖ Confusing marginal with conditional: $p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$ averages over $z$ (the latent component). It is NOT $p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid z=k, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ — that would be just a single Gaussian.

▶ Real-World Applications
Anomaly Detection Points with very low $p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$ are anomalies — they don't fit any component well.
Speech Recognition Each phoneme is a Gaussian component; the marginal density scores how likely an audio frame is.
Image Segmentation Each color cluster is a Gaussian; $p(\mathbf{x})$ tells how well a pixel belongs to the overall model.
Customer Segmentation Each customer segment has its own spending distribution; $p(\mathbf{x})$ gives the likelihood of a purchase pattern.
03

The full likelihood over all data

Since observations are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), the joint likelihood of the entire dataset is the product of every point's marginal density.

Equation (2)
Full Likelihood
$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \left[ \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2) \right]$$
A product over $N$ data points — each term is itself a sum over $K$ components
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The likelihood of the parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ given all data is the product over $i$ from $1$ to $N$ of [the sum over $k$ from $1$ to $K$ of $\pi_k$ times the Gaussian density of $\mathbf{x}_i$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ and variance $\sigma_k^2$].”
▶ What's New vs. Equation (1)
SymbolMeaningRole
$\displaystyle\prod_{i=1}^{N}$ Product over all $N$ data points $N = 100 \implies$ multiply 100 terms
$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ Likelihood function — probability of seeing ALL the data $p(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$
▶ Intuition
If you flip a coin twice, the probability of seeing [Heads, Tails] is $P(\text{Heads}) \times P(\text{Tails})$. The likelihood works the same way: because each data point is independent of the others, the overall probability of seeing all $N$ points together is simply the product of their individual probabilities.

The catch is that each individual probability is itself a sum over $K$ components — so instead of a product of simple terms, we get a product of sums. This nested structure is what makes GMM optimization hard.
▶ Step-by-Step Construction
1
Compute the marginal density for each data point
$p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2)$
↓
2
Repeat for $i = 1, 2, \dots, N$ — giving $N$ separate values
$p(\mathbf{x}_1), p(\mathbf{x}_2), \dots, p(\mathbf{x}_N)$
↓
3
Multiply all $N$ values together (i.i.d. assumption)
$\displaystyle \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^N p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$
▶ Numerical Example ($N = 3$ points, $K = 2$)
Setup: Three data points: $\mathbf{x} = [170, 175, 165]$. Same GMM as before ($\boldsymbol{\pi} = [0.6, 0.4]$, $\boldsymbol{\mu} = [170, 165]$, $\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2 = [25, 36]$).
Point $\mathbf{x}_i$$p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$Calculation
$170$ $0.0798$ $0.6 \cdot 0.0798 + 0.4 \cdot 0.0337 = 0.0479 + 0.0135$
$175$ $0.0528$ $0.6 \cdot 0.0514 + 0.4 \cdot 0.0236 = 0.0308 + 0.0118$
$165$ $0.0469$ $0.6 \cdot 0.0337 + 0.4 \cdot 0.0665 = 0.0202 + 0.0266$
$\displaystyle \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = 0.0798 \times 0.0528 \times 0.0469 = \mathbf{0.000198}$
i
What the likelihood value means

A likelihood of 0.000198 is very small — but that's expected when multiplying many densities (each can be $< 1$). What matters is relative comparison: a different set of parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}'$ that gives $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}') = 0.0005$ is a 2.5× better fit. In practice we work with log-likelihood (next section) to avoid numerical underflow from multiplying many small numbers.

!
Common Mistakes

✖ Adding instead of multiplying: For i.i.d. data, the joint probability is the product of individual probabilities, not the sum. $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$, not $\sum p(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$.

✖ Forgetting that each term is a sum: The product is over $N$ terms, and each term is already a sum over $K$ components. This is not the same as $\prod_i \sum_k = \sum_k \prod_i$ — you can't swap the order.

04

The log-likelihood bottleneck

Taking the log usually turns a product into a sum — easy to differentiate. But for GMMs, the sum inside each term survives the log, blocking the simplification.

Equation (3)
Log-Likelihood
$$\log \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log\!\left[ \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2) \right]$$
The log moves past the product but cannot enter the inner sum
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The log-likelihood of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is the sum over $i$ from $1$ to $N$ of the log of [the sum over $k$ from $1$ to $K$ of $\pi_k$ times the Gaussian density of $\mathbf{x}_i$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ and variance $\sigma_k^2$].”
▶ Key Difference from Single Gaussian
CaseLog-likelihood formCan we isolate parameters?
Single Gaussian $(K=1)$ $\displaystyle\sum_i \log \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \sigma^2)$ ✔ Yes
GMM $(K > 1)$ $\displaystyle\sum_i \log\!\left( \sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2) \right)$ ✘ No
▶ Intuition
The log has a superpower: $\log(a \times b) = \log a + \log b$. This turns products into sums, which are easy to take derivatives of.

But the log does not help with sums: $\log(a + b) \neq \log a + \log b$. Inside the GMM log-likelihood, the sum over components is trapped inside the log, making it impossible to separate one component's parameters from another's by taking derivatives.
▶ Step-by-Step Derivation: Likelihood → Log-Likelihood
1
Start with the likelihood (product over points)
$\displaystyle \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^N \bigl[ \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2) \bigr]$
↓ Take $\log$ of both sides
2
Apply the log rule: $\log(\prod a_i) = \sum \log a_i$
$\displaystyle \log \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log\!\bigl[ \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2) \bigr]$
↓ We want to push $\log$ inside the sum ...
3
But $\log(a + b) \neq \log a + \log b$ — blocked!
$\displaystyle \log\!\bigl( \sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}_k \bigr) \neq \sum_k \log(\pi_k \mathcal{N}_k)$
↓ The inner sum survives the log
4
Result: a log-likelihood with a trapped inner sum
$\displaystyle \log \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log\!\bigl( \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_i \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \sigma_k^2) \bigr) \longleftarrow \text{bottleneck}$
▶ Visual: The Log-Sum Bottleneck
The log glides through the product but slams into the inner sum — this is the core difficulty of GMM optimization.

!
Why this blocks everything downstream

$\log(a + b) \neq \log a + \log b$. The mixture's inner sum survives the log untouched, so the neat term-by-term derivative that worked for a single Gaussian no longer isolates any single $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$, $\sigma_k^2$, or $\pi_k$.

05

Why MLE stalls here

Two compounding difficulties keep the GMM log-likelihood from being solved the way we solved a single Gaussian.

No closed-form solution

Setting $\partial \log \mathcal{L} / \partial \boldsymbol{\theta} = 0$ no longer isolates a clean formula (like the sample mean). Each parameter's equation stays entangled with every other component through the trapped inner sum.

Constrained optimization

The mixing proportions must satisfy $\sum \pi_k = 1$ and $\pi_k \ge 0$. Any optimizer has to respect that constraint on every step, not just find an unconstrained maximum.
