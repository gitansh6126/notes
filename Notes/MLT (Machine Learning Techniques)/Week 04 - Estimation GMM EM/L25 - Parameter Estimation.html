The Expectation–Maximization Framework

A log inside a sum you can maximize. A sum inside a log, you can't. This lecture is about the trick that turns the second kind of problem back into the first — by inventing a set of numbers that don't even appear in the original question.

the wall log L(θ)  =  Σi=1n log ( Σk=1K πk · 𝒩(xi | μk, σk²) )
01

Why This Likelihood Fights Back

Ordinary maximum likelihood estimation works because the log of a product becomes a friendly sum of logs, and sums differentiate cleanly. A Gaussian Mixture Model breaks that pattern before you even get started: inside every term, there's already a sum — over clusters — sitting underneath the log.

⚠️
The obstruction Take the derivative of log(π1𝒩1 + π2𝒩2 + …) with respect to μk, and every single term in the mixture drags every other term into the denominator. There is no clean algebraic split — no closed-form solution falls out. The parameters for every cluster are tangled together inside one log.

The Two Teachers, One Gradebook

Picture a stack of 25 exam papers from an intro class secretly co-taught by two professors with very different grading styles. Professor Gold grades tough and centers around the low 60s. Professor Blue is generous and centers in the high 80s. The papers are shuffled — no names, no section numbers.

You only see the final scores. You don't know which professor graded which paper. Yet you want to estimate both professors' average and spread, and what fraction of the class each of them taught — all from the anonymous scores alone.

This is exactly the GMM setup: each score xi was generated by one of K Gaussians, but the label zi telling you which one is latent — hidden from the data. That hidden label is the entire source of the summation trapped inside the log.

02

Inventing a Confession: λik

Since we can't observe which professor graded which paper, we introduce an artificial number for every (paper, professor) pair that guesses at the answer — not with a hard yes/no, but with a soft probability.

📐
Definition For every data point i and every cluster k, define λik — how much we currently believe paper i belongs to professor k. These aren't real, observed quantities. They're auxiliary parameters we get to invent purely to make the math tractable.
Constraints on λ
Σk=1K λik = 1      0 ≤ λik ≤ 1
For each student i, their "belief mass" across all K professors adds up to exactly 1 — a full probability distribution over who graded their paper.

Reading a single λ

If λ7,1 = 0.9 and λ7,2 = 0.1, that means: given what we currently know, student 7's paper is 90% likely to have come from Professor Gold's stack, and only 10% likely to be Professor Blue's. Extreme scores near 60 or 90 get confident, lopsided λ's; ambiguous scores near 74 sit close to 50/50.

03

Jensen's Inequality: A Bound You Can Actually Climb

Here's the payoff for inventing λ. Because log is a concave function, Jensen's Inequality guarantees that the log of an average is always at least as large as the average of the logs. Plugging in λ as weights lets us pull the log inside the sum over k — turning an intractable expression into a lower bound we can actually differentiate.

The gold curve is the true, unwieldy log-likelihood. The blue dashed chord is the lower bound Jensen's Inequality gives us — always resting at or below the curve, and touching it exactly at the current λ.

💡
Hill-climbing in the fog Imagine climbing a mountain (the true log-likelihood) in thick fog — you can't see its real shape. But at your current position, you can construct a flat platform that touches the mountain exactly where you stand and never pokes above it. Climb to the highest point of that platform, and you're guaranteed to be at least as high on the real mountain too. That platform is the Jensen lower bound; each E-step rebuilds it, each M-step climbs it.
The modified objective — a maximizable lower bound
Σi=1n Σk=1K λik · log ( πk 𝒩(xi | μk, σk²)λik )
The log now sits directly next to a single Gaussian term — no sum trapped underneath it. This is what makes closed-form derivatives possible.
04

The Key Insight: Split the Problem in Two

The modified objective still has two families of unknowns tangled together: the λ's and θ = (μ, σ², π). The trick is to never solve for both at once. Instead, freeze one, solve the other exactly, then swap. Each half turns out to have a clean, closed-form answer.

M-step: Fixing λ, Solving for θ

With λik held fixed, the closed-form solutions look almost identical to ordinary MLE for a single Gaussian — except every term now carries a λik weight, so points more "confidently" assigned to cluster k pull its estimate harder.

Weighted mean
μk = Σi λik xiΣi λik
Weighted variance
σk² = Σi λik (xi − μk)²Σi λik
Mixing weight
πk = Σi λikn
The expected fraction of all n students that belong to cluster k.

E-step: Fixing θ, Solving for λ

With θ held fixed, each student's λik is solved on its own — completely independent of every other student. The answer is just an application of Bayes' Theorem: given the score xi and the current professor profiles, what's the posterior probability of each professor?

Responsibility via Bayes' Theorem
λik = P(zi = k | xi, θ) = πk 𝒩(xi | μk, σk²)Σj πj 𝒩(xi | μj, σj²)
Numerator: how plausible is score xi under professor k's profile, weighted by how common that professor is. Denominator: normalize across all professors so the λ's sum to 1.
💡
Why this is Bayes, not a guess "Responsibility" is just posterior probability wearing a different name. Before seeing the score (the prior) we believe cluster k occurs with probability πk. After seeing the score xi (the likelihood 𝒩(xi|μk,σk²)), Bayes' rule updates that belief into λik — the posterior.

06

Where This Leaves Us

TL;DR — Setting the Stage for EM

  • The problem: a sum inside a log makes direct MLE on a mixture model intractable — no closed form, tangled derivatives.
  • The trick: invent responsibilities λik, and use Jensen's Inequality to build a tight lower bound that can be maximized directly.
  • The split: alternate between two easy sub-problems — M-step (weighted mean/variance/proportion, given λ) and E-step (Bayes'-rule posterior, given θ).
  • The guarantee: because each step only ever climbs the current lower bound, the true log-likelihood never decreases across iterations.
  • What's next: formalize this loop as the full Expectation-Maximization algorithm, with a convergence criterion and a proof that it never gets worse.
