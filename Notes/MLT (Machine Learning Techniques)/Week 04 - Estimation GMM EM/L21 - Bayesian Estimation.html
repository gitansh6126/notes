Bayesian Estimation

MLE only listens to the evidence in front of it. Bayesian estimation lets you walk in with a hunch — and shows you exactly how much the evidence should change your mind.

§1 Where MLE Leaves Off §2 Bayes' Theorem §3 Beta-Bernoulli §4 Conjugacy §5 Point Estimates
01

Where MLE leaves off

MLE treats the true parameter as fixed and unknown, and lets the data speak entirely for itself. Bayesian estimation takes a different stance: it treats the parameter itself as uncertain, and lets you formally combine two sources of information — what you believed before seeing the data, and what the data itself tells you.

$P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$

Prior

Your belief about $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ before seeing any data.

$P(\text{data} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$

Likelihood

How probable the data is for a given $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ (same as MLE).

$P(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \text{data})$

Posterior

Your updated belief after combining prior with evidence.

▶ MLE vs. Bayesian Philosophy
MLE collapses to a single number. Bayesian inference keeps a full distribution, preserving uncertainty.
i
Detective analogy

In the MLE case, the detective judged suspects purely by how well their story matched the evidence. A Bayesian detective also weighs each suspect's prior reputation — a known conman starts more suspicious than a lifelong monk, even before either says a word.

02

Bayes' theorem

The mathematical engine of Bayesian inference is Bayes' Theorem. It tells us exactly how to update the prior into the posterior using the likelihood.

Equation (1)
Bayes' Theorem
$$P(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \frac{P(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) \, P(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{P(\mathbf{x})}$$
The posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The posterior probability of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ given the data equals the likelihood of the data given $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, multiplied by the prior probability of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, divided by the marginal probability of the data (which normalizes everything to sum to 1).”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningRole
$P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ Prior — belief about $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ before data What you knew (or assumed) going in
$P(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})$ Likelihood — probability of data given $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ Same as MLE's likelihood function
$P(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{x})$ Posterior — updated belief after seeing data The goal of Bayesian inference
$P(\mathbf{x})$ Evidence / marginal likelihood — normalizing constant $\int P(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) P(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \, d\boldsymbol{\theta}$
▶ Intuition
Since $P(\mathbf{x})$ is constant with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, we usually work with the simpler proportionality: $\text{Posterior} \propto \text{Likelihood} \times \text{Prior}$.

Think of it as a weighted average: the prior is your starting point, and the likelihood pulls you toward the data. The more data you have, the more the likelihood dominates. With infinite data, the prior's influence vanishes, and the posterior mode converges to the MLE.
▶ Bayes Flow: Prior → Posterior
$P(\mathbf{x})$ is a normalizing constant — it ensures the posterior integrates to 1. The core update is Prior $\times$ Likelihood.
Equation (2)
Posterior Proportionality
$$P(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto P(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) \, P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$
The denominator $P(\mathbf{x})$ does not depend on $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, so we drop it for optimization
03

Worked example — Bernoulli + Beta

Estimating a coin's bias $p$ again — but this time with a starting hunch, encoded as a Beta distribution over $[0, 1]$.

Equation (3)
Beta Prior
$$P(p) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} \, p^{\alpha - 1} (1 - p)^{\beta - 1}$$
The Beta distribution is a flexible prior over $p \in [0, 1]$, parameterized by $\alpha, \beta > 0$
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The prior density of $p$ is proportional to $p^{\alpha - 1} (1 - p)^{\beta - 1}$, normalized by the Beta function $B(\alpha, \beta)$. Larger $\alpha$ biases toward $p = 1$; larger $\beta$ biases toward $p = 0$. Equal $\alpha = \beta$ gives a symmetric prior centered at $0.5$.”
▶ Beta Distribution Parameters
ParameterMeaningEffect on Prior
$\alpha$ Pseudo-count of "heads" before seeing data $\alpha = 3$ means 3 imaginary heads
$\beta$ Pseudo-count of "tails" before seeing data $\beta = 3$ means 3 imaginary tails
$B(\alpha, \beta)$ Beta function — normalizing constant Ensures the density integrates to 1
▶ Beta-Bernoulli Posterior Derivation
1
Start with the prior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$
$P(p) \propto p^{\alpha - 1}(1-p)^{\beta - 1}$
↓ Multiply by Bernoulli likelihood
2
Likelihood: $P(\mathbf{x} \mid p) = p^{n_h} (1-p)^{N - n_h}$
$P(p \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto p^{\alpha + n_h - 1} (1-p)^{\beta + N - n_h - 1}$
↓ Recognize the form — it is Beta again!
3
Posterior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha + n_h, \beta + N - n_h)$
$P(p \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto p^{\alpha' - 1} (1-p)^{\beta' - 1}$
▶ Numerical Example
Setup: Prior $\text{Beta}(3, 3)$ (fair prior). 8 flips: 5 heads, 3 tails.
QuantityExpressionValue
Prior $\alpha, \beta$ $(3, 3)$ "Ghost" 3 heads, 3 tails
Observed heads $n_h = 5$ 5 real heads
Observed tails $n_t = 3$ 3 real tails
Posterior $\alpha', \beta'$ $(3+5, 3+3) = (8, 6)$ $\text{Beta}(8, 6)$
$\displaystyle \text{Posterior} = \text{Beta}(8, 6)$ — posterior mean $= \frac{8}{8+6} \approx 0.571$
▶ Intuition
The Beta prior can be interpreted as "ghost data" — imaginary observations that you've already seen before the real experiment. A $\text{Beta}(3, 3)$ prior behaves like having secretly seen 3 heads and 3 tails already.

When you add 5 real heads and 3 real tails, the total "evidence" is $3 + 5 = 8$ heads and $3 + 3 = 6$ tails — hence $\text{Beta}(8, 6)$. This is the magic of conjugacy: the posterior stays in the same family as the prior, and the update is just adding counts.
04

Conjugacy

A prior is conjugate to a likelihood when the posterior belongs to the same distribution family as the prior. Conjugacy keeps Bayesian updates closed-form and computationally cheap.

Equation (4)
Conjugate Update Rule
$$\text{Prior: } \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta) \;\longrightarrow\; \text{Posterior: } \text{Beta}(\alpha + n_h, \beta + n_t)$$
For Bernoulli likelihood, the Beta prior is conjugate — posterior is Beta with updated parameters
▶ Common Conjugate Pairs
Beta ↔ Bernoulli Prior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$
Likelihood: $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$
Posterior: $\text{Beta}(\alpha + n_h, \beta + n_t)$
Normal ↔ Normal Prior: $\N(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$
Likelihood: $\N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ (known $\sigma^2$)
Posterior: $\N(\mu_n, \sigma_n^2)$
Gamma ↔ Poisson Prior: $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$
Likelihood: $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$
Posterior: $\text{Gamma}(\alpha + \sum x_i, \beta + N)$
Dirichlet ↔ Multinomial Prior: $\text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$
Likelihood: $\text{Multinomial}(\mathbf{x})$
Posterior: $\text{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha} + \mathbf{x})$
✔
Why conjugacy matters

Without conjugacy, computing the posterior requires expensive numerical integration or sampling (MCMC). With conjugacy, the update is a simple formula — enabling fast, closed-form Bayesian inference.

05

Point estimation from the posterior

A full posterior distribution is the richest answer — but sometimes you need one number. Two standard ways to collapse it:

$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MAP}}$

MAP Estimate

The mode of the posterior — the single $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ with the highest posterior density. This is the Bayesian analogue of MLE.

$\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{x}]$

Posterior Mean

The expected value of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ under the posterior. For symmetric unimodal posteriors, this equals the mode.

$\text{Credible Interval}$

Uncertainty Quantification

A region containing $95\%$ of the posterior mass — unlike confidence intervals, these have a direct probability interpretation.

Equation (5)
Maximum a Posteriori (MAP)
$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} P(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} P(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) \, P(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$
MAP = MLE with a prior regularizer — the prior pulls the estimate toward its own center
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The MAP estimate is the value of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ that maximizes the posterior. Because the denominator $P(\mathbf{x})$ does not depend on $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, this is equivalent to maximizing the likelihood times the prior. When the prior is uniform (flat), MAP = MLE.”
▶ Intuition
Both MAP and expected value are shortcuts. The posterior distribution itself is the real result — MAP and the expected value just compress it down to a single verdict when a decision has to be made.

The real strength of Bayesian methods is carrying a full distribution over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ — keeping track of how confident you should be, not just what your best guess is. That uncertainty-awareness is exactly what later gets reused inside probabilistic machine learning methods, including Gaussian Mixture Models.
▶ MAP vs. Posterior Mean for Beta-Bernoulli
Setup: $\text{Beta}(8, 6)$ posterior from earlier example.
EstimatorFormulaValue
MAP (mode) $\displaystyle \frac{\alpha' - 1}{\alpha' + \beta' - 2}$ $\displaystyle \frac{8-1}{8+6-2} = \frac{7}{12} \approx 0.583$
Posterior Mean $\displaystyle \frac{\alpha'}{\alpha' + \beta'}$ $\displaystyle \frac{8}{8+6} = \frac{8}{14} \approx 0.571$
MLE $\displaystyle \frac{n_h}{N}$ $\displaystyle \frac{5}{8} = 0.625$
The prior pulls the MAP and mean away from the MLE toward the prior's center ($0.5$)
→
Next steps

Bayesian methods give a principled way to blend expert knowledge with fresh evidence. By carrying a full distribution over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ instead of collapsing straight to one number, Bayesian estimation keeps track of how confident you should be — exactly what later gets reused inside GMMs.
