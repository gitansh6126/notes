Reopening the Case

Where MLE leaves off

MLE treats the true parameter as fixed and unknown, and lets the data speak entirely for itself. Bayesian estimation takes a different stance: it treats the parameter itself as uncertain, and lets you formally combine two sources of information — what you believed before seeing the data, and what the data itself tells you.

🕵️
Detective analogy, continuedIn the MLE case file, the detective judged suspects purely by how well their story matched the evidence. A Bayesian detective also weighs each suspect's prior reputation — a known conman starts more suspicious than a lifelong monk, even before either says a word.

Key Concepts

Concept A

θ as a Random Variable

Frequentist methods (like MLE) treat θ as one fixed, unknown constant. Bayesian statistics instead treats θ as a random variable, described by its own probability distribution that can be updated as evidence arrives.

Concept B

Prior, P(θ)

Your belief about θ before seeing any data — domain knowledge, past experience, or a reasonable hunch, encoded as a distribution.

Concept C

Likelihood, P(data | θ)

The same quantity from the MLE case file: how probable the observed data is, for a given value of θ.

Concept D

Posterior, P(θ | data)

Your updated belief about θ, after the prior and the likelihood have been formally combined. This is the payoff of the whole method.

Point Estimation from the Posterior

Closing the File to a Single Number

A full posterior distribution is the richest answer — but sometimes you need one number. Two standard ways to collapse it:

MethodDefinitionReads as
MAP The mode — the θ with the single highest posterior density Most likely value
Expected Value The mean of the posterior distribution Average belief
Both are shortcuts. The posterior distribution itself is the real result — MAP and the expected value just compress it down to a single verdict when a decision has to be made.
Case Summary

Why keep the whole distribution?

Bayesian methods give a principled way to blend expert knowledge with fresh evidence, rather than treating every estimate as though it arrived from nowhere. By carrying a full distribution over θ — instead of collapsing straight to one number, the way MLE does — Bayesian estimation keeps track of how confident you should be, not just what your best guess is. That uncertainty-awareness is exactly what later gets reused inside probabilistic machine learning methods, including clustering.
