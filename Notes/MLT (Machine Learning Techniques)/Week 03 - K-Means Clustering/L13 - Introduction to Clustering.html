Introduction to
Clustering

PCA and Kernel PCA both asked "what directions or surfaces does this data lie along?" Clustering asks a completely different question: forget directions — where are the natural groups?

Paradigm · Unsupervised learning Core idea · Partition data into homogeneous groups Hard part · Exponentially many possible partitions
1

Overview & Motivation

A DIFFERENT QUESTION

Not "which direction," but "which group"

PCA and Kernel PCA are forms of representation learning — they look for linear subspaces, or directions of maximum variance, that summarize the data. Clustering asks something else entirely: even data that already lies neatly within a subspace often forms distinct clumps within it. Those clumps carry structural information that variance-hunting alone will never reveal.

Real-life analogy

Imagine a jar of mixed candy — all roughly the same size and shape (so no "direction of variance" separates them), but clearly made up of a few different flavors sitting in loose piles. Nobody labeled the piles for you. Clustering is the act of sorting the jar by flavor using nothing but how the candies look and taste next to their neighbors — no labels, no instructions, just noticing what's alike.

The Goal of Clustering

THE SETUP

Partition n points into K boxes

Given n data points x₁, ..., xₙ living in d-dimensional space, the goal is to sort them into K distinct groups — think of it as assigning each point to one of K labeled boxes.

n points, sorted into K = 3 boxes
Cluster 1 Cluster 2 Cluster 3

Solid dots are data points; the ringed markers are each cluster's mean. There's no rule handed to us for where the boundaries go — we have to infer them from how the points naturally clump.

Combinatorial complexity

Assigning n points to K groups has roughly Kn possible outcomes — each of the n points independently could go into any of K boxes. This makes finding the best partition by checking every option NP-hard: computationally infeasible once n grows past a tiny handful of points.

Real-life analogy

Picture a wedding planner seating n guests at K tables, free to put any guest at any table. With just 10 guests and 3 tables, there are already 310 ≈ 59,000 possible seating charts. Checking every single one to find the "best" arrangement by brute force stops being realistic almost immediately — and real datasets have far more than 10 points.

Defining a Performance Measure

HOW DO WE SCORE A PARTITION?

Cluster indicators, and the homogeneity idea

Since we can't check every partition, we need a numeric score to tell a good partition from a bad one — something we can eventually search for efficiently. We introduce cluster indicator variables z₁, ..., zₙ, where zᵢ ∈ {1, ..., K} records which cluster point xᵢ is assigned to.

A good partition is homogeneous: points inside a cluster should look alike. We measure "alikeness" indirectly — by how close each point sits to its cluster's own center.

Real-life analogy

Think of a group of friends scattered across a city trying to pick one meeting spot each for a few separate hangouts. A "good" choice of meeting spots is one where everyone in each hangout group lives reasonably close to their group's chosen spot — not one where people have to trek across town. The meeting spot is the cluster's center; the total walking distance is exactly the kind of thing we're about to define mathematically.

The cluster mean

The center of cluster k is just the average of every point currently assigned to it:

μk  =  Σᵢ xᵢ · 𝟙(zᵢ = k)  ⁄  Σᵢ 𝟙(zᵢ = k) numerator: sum of points currently in cluster k · denominator: how many points that is — an ordinary average

The objective function

Putting it together: we want to choose assignments z₁, ..., zₙ that minimize the total squared distance from every point to its own cluster's mean — this total is often called the within-cluster sum of squares:

Minimize   Σᵢ₌₁n ||xi − μzi||2 sum, over every point, of its squared distance to whichever cluster mean it's currently assigned to
Key insight

This objective quietly does two things at once: it depends on where the means are, and on which points are assigned where — and each of those depends on the other. That circularity is exactly what future algorithms (like k-means) are built to untangle.

Algorithmic Challenges

WHY WE CAN'T JUST CHECK EVERYTHING

The search space explodes long before n gets "large"

Because the number of candidate partitions grows like Kn, exhaustive search is only ever an option for toy-sized problems. The chart below fixes K = 3 clusters and shows how fast the count of possible partitions runs away as n grows.

Bars are compressed to fit the page — by n = 50, the true count of possible partitions is comparable to the number of molecules in a handful of table salt. No computer checks all of those one by one.

Worth noting

"NP-hard" doesn't mean impossible — it means there's no known method that finds the guaranteed-best partition quickly for all inputs. It doesn't stop us from finding very good partitions efficiently; it just rules out brute-force certainty.

Real-life analogy

It's the same wall a delivery driver hits trying to find the absolute shortest route through 50 stops: checking every possible order is hopeless, but a sensible heuristic — like always driving to the nearest unvisited stop next — gets you a route that's very good, very fast, even without a guarantee it's the mathematical best.

Looking ahead

Since exhaustive search is off the table, the next lecture introduces k-means — a heuristic algorithm that alternates between assigning points to their nearest mean and recomputing the means, converging quickly to a good (if not always globally optimal) partition.

Key Takeaways

1

Clustering finds group structure, not directions of variance — a fundamentally different unsupervised goal than PCA.

2

Partitioning n points into K clusters has ~K^n possible outcomes, making exhaustive search NP-hard.

3

Cluster indicators z_i and cluster means μ_k let us score any given partition by within-cluster squared distance.

4

Because we can't search exhaustively, upcoming lectures turn to heuristics like k-means for a fast, good-enough solution.
