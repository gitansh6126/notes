01

Overview & Motivation

Clustering finds group structure, not directions of variance — a fundamentally different unsupervised goal than PCA.

▶ A Different Question
PCA and Kernel PCA are forms of representation learning — they look for linear subspaces, or directions of maximum variance, that summarize the data. Clustering asks something else entirely: even data that already lies neatly within a subspace often forms distinct clumps within it. Those clumps carry structural information that variance-hunting alone will never reveal.
▶ Real-Life Analogy
Imagine a jar of mixed candy — all roughly the same size and shape (so no "direction of variance" separates them), but clearly made up of a few different flavors sitting in loose piles. Nobody labeled the piles for you. Clustering is the act of sorting the jar by flavor using nothing but how the candies look and taste next to their neighbors — no labels, no instructions, just noticing what's alike.

02

The Goal of Clustering

Given $n$ data points $\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the goal is to sort them into $K$ distinct groups — assigning each point to one of $K$ labeled boxes.

Equation (1)
Partitioning Problem
$$\text{Find } z_1, \dots, z_n \in \{1, \dots, K\} \text{ that optimize a scoring function}$$
The cluster assignment variables $z_i$ encode which group each point belongs to
▶ Read the Setup in English
“We have $n$ points. We want to assign each one a label $z_i$ from $1$ to $K$. The assignment should make points within each label look alike and points across labels look different.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$n$ Number of data points $n = 100$
$d$ Dimensionality of each point $d = 3$ (height, width, depth)
$K$ Number of clusters to find $K = 3$ groups
$z_i$ Cluster indicator for point $i$ $z_5 = 2$
$\mathbf{x}_i$ The $i$-th data point $\mathbf{x}_1 = [1.2, 3.4]^T$
▶ Intuition
Picture a wedding planner seating $n$ guests at $K$ tables, free to put any guest at any table. With just 10 guests and 3 tables, there are already $3^{10} \approx 59{,}000$ possible seating charts. Checking every single one to find the "best" arrangement by brute force stops being realistic almost immediately — and real datasets have far more than 10 points.
▶ Schematic: Partitioning $n$ Points Into $K$ Groups
The clustering pipeline: raw points $\rightarrow$ assignment variables $\rightarrow$ partitioned groups
03

Defining a Performance Measure

Since we can't check every partition, we need a numeric score to tell a good partition from a bad one — something we can eventually search for efficiently.

▶ Cluster Indicators & Homogeneity
A good partition is homogeneous: points inside a cluster should look alike. We measure "alikeness" indirectly — by how close each point sits to its cluster's own center.
Equation (2)
Cluster Mean
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{n_k} \sum_{i : z_i = k} \mathbf{x}_i \quad \text{where} \quad n_k = \bigl|\{i : z_i = k\}\bigr|$$
The center of cluster $k$ is the arithmetic mean of all points assigned to it
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ of cluster $k$ equals the sum of all points assigned to cluster $k$, divided by $n_k$, the number of points in that cluster.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ Mean (centroid) of cluster $k$ $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 = [1.5, 2.3]^T$
$n_k$ Number of points in cluster $k$ $n_1 = 42$
$\displaystyle\sum_{i : z_i = k}$ Sum over points assigned to cluster $k$ Sum of 42 vectors
Equation (3)
Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS)
$$\text{WCSS} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_i}\|^2$$
The total squared distance from every point to its own cluster's mean — the objective to minimize
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The within-cluster sum of squares is the sum over each point of the squared distance between that point and the mean of the cluster it's assigned to. This is the total cost we want to minimize.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_i}\|^2$ Squared Euclidean distance from point $i$ to its cluster's mean $\|x - \mu\|^2 = 4.2$
$\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}$ Sum over all $n$ data points $n = 100$ terms
▶ Intuition
Think of a group of friends scattered across a city trying to pick one meeting spot each for a few separate hangouts. A "good" choice of meeting spots is one where everyone in each hangout group lives reasonably close to their group's chosen spot — not one where people have to trek across town. The meeting spot is the cluster's center; the total walking distance is exactly the WCSS.
▶ Step-by-Step Construction of WCSS
1
Assign each point to a cluster — get $z_i$ for each $i$
$z_i \in \{1,\dots,K\}$
↓
2
Compute the mean of each cluster
$\displaystyle \boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{n_k} \sum_{i:z_i=k} \mathbf{x}_i$
↓
3
Measure each point's squared distance to its own mean
$\|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_i}\|^2$
↓
4
Sum over all points to get the total cost
$\displaystyle \text{WCSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_i}\|^2$

i
What WCSS measures

A lower WCSS means points are tightly packed around their means — a good clustering. But WCSS always decreases as $K$ increases (more clusters = tighter fit), which is why we can't just minimize WCSS alone. That's the topic of choosing $K$.

→
Key insight

This objective quietly does two things at once: it depends on where the means are, and on which points are assigned where — and each of those depends on the other. That circularity is exactly what future algorithms (like K-Means) are built to untangle.

04

Algorithmic Challenges

The number of candidate partitions grows like $K^n$, making exhaustive search NP-hard.

Equation (4)
Combinatorial Explosion
$$\# \text{partitions} = K^{\,n}$$
Each of $n$ points can independently go into any of $K$ clusters — exponential growth
▶ Why Exhaustive Search Is Impossible
With $K = 3$ and $n = 50$, there are $3^{50} \approx 7.2 \times 10^{23}$ possible partitions — comparable to the number of molecules in a handful of table salt. No computer can check them all.
▶ Growth of $K^n$ for $K = 3$
$n$$3^n$Feasibility
$5$$243$Trivial
$10$$\approx 59{,}000$Tractable
$20$$\approx 3.5 \times 10^9$Infeasible
$50$$\approx 7.2 \times 10^{23}$Impossible
▶ Intuition
"NP-hard" doesn't mean impossible — it means there's no known method that finds the guaranteed-best partition quickly for all inputs. It doesn't stop us from finding very good partitions efficiently; it just rules out brute-force certainty. It's the same wall a delivery driver hits trying to find the absolute shortest route through 50 stops: checking every possible order is hopeless, but a sensible heuristic — like always driving to the nearest unvisited stop next — gets you a route that's very good, very fast.
→
Looking ahead

Since exhaustive search is off the table, the next lecture introduces K-Means (Lloyd's algorithm) — a heuristic algorithm that alternates between assigning points to their nearest mean and recomputing the means, converging quickly to a good (if not always globally optimal) partition.

05

Key Takeaways

Clustering ≠ PCA

Clustering finds group structure, not directions of variance — a fundamentally different unsupervised goal.

NP-Hard

Partitioning $n$ points into $K$ clusters has $\sim K^n$ possible outcomes, making exhaustive search hopeless.

WCSS Objective

Cluster indicators $z_i$ and cluster means $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ let us score any given partition by within-cluster squared distance.

→
Next up

Because we can't search exhaustively, upcoming lectures turn to heuristics like K-Means (Lloyd's algorithm) for a fast, good-enough solution.
