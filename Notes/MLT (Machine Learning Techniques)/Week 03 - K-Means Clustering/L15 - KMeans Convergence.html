Convergence of K-Means —
Why Lloyd's Algorithm
Always Stops

K-Means alternates between two simple steps — assign points, then recompute means — and just... stops. Nobody tells it when. This lecture proves that it has to, explains why the resting point it finds isn't always the best one, and unpacks what that means for how you actually run the algorithm.

The Questions On the Table

SETTING THE STAGE

K-Means just... stops. Should we trust that?

Run Lloyd's algorithm on any dataset and, sooner or later, an iteration passes where nothing changes — no point switches clusters, the means stop moving. Before relying on that behavior, four questions deserve real answers:

  • Convergence — is that stopping point guaranteed, or could the algorithm loop forever?
  • Cluster quality — when it does stop, how good are the clusters it hands back?
  • Initialization — does where we start the cluster centers change where we end up?
  • Choosing K — with no labels to check against, how do we even pick the number of clusters?

This lecture answers the first two head-on with a proof, and the proof itself explains why the last two matter as much as they do.

2

The Fundamental Fact: The Mean Is the Best Meeting Point

THE LOAD-BEARING LEMMA

Of every possible "center," the mean minimizes total squared distance

Everything in this lecture rests on one geometric fact. Given any fixed set of points X₁, X₂, ..., XL ∈ ℝd, ask: which single point V minimizes the sum of squared distances to all of them? The answer is always their mean:

V*  =  argminV Σi=1L ‖Xi − V‖²   =   1/L Σi=1L Xi differentiate the objective with respect to V, set the gradient to zero — the mean falls straight out

No other candidate point — not the median, not a randomly chosen member of the set — beats the mean on this specific criterion. That specificity matters: it's exactly the criterion K-Means is built around.

Real-life analogy

Picture choosing a meeting spot for a group of friends scattered across a city, where the "cost" of the meetup is the sum of everyone's squared travel distance. You wouldn't pick city hall or a random friend's house — you'd pick the location that balances everyone's position, which turns out to be their average location. That balancing point, and nothing else, minimizes the total squared travel.

Why squared, specifically

This clean result depends on the distance being squared. It's the algebraic reason K-Means uses squared Euclidean distance throughout, rather than, say, plain distance — the mean would no longer be the guaranteed minimizer otherwise.

3

Proof of Convergence, Step by Step

THE SETUP

Two moving parts, updated in alternation

At iteration T, the algorithm's entire state is a pair: cluster assignments ZT (which cluster each point belongs to) and cluster means μT (the center of each cluster). One full iteration updates them in strict alternation:

A

Reassign

Every point moves to whichever existing mean μT is closest to it in squared distance. A point only moves if some other cluster's mean is strictly closer than its current one.

B

Update means

Given the freshly reassigned clusters, recompute each cluster's mean μT+1 — using exactly the fact from Section 2.

The red-ringed points switched from the amber cluster to the blue one because the blue mean became the closer option. Both means then shift to sit at the true center of their (now-updated) membership.

THE ARGUMENT

Why the objective can only go down

Track the K-Means objective — total squared distance from every point to its own cluster's mean — across one full iteration, in two halves:

  1. The reassignment half never increases the objective. Each point either keeps its current mean, or switches to a mean that is strictly closer. Either way, no point's individual contribution to the total can go up.
  2. The mean-update half never increases it either. By the Fundamental Fact, μT+1 is the single best possible center for the newly reassigned points — strictly better than (or, at worst, tied with) using the old means μT to score those same points.
Objective(ZT+1, μT+1)  ≤  Objective(ZT+1, μT)  ≤  Objective(ZT, μT) reassigning can only help (right inequality); re-centering with the true mean can only help further (left inequality)

Chain the two halves together and the conclusion is unavoidable: the objective is monotonically non-increasing, iteration after iteration.

The objective across iterations — never climbs back up

Each bar is the total squared distance after one full iteration. It keeps falling — sometimes in big drops, sometimes barely at all — until an iteration changes nothing and the bar flattens out (green).

Real-life analogy

It's like water finding its level: every point flowing to a lower spot only ever lowers the total, never raises it. The system might settle quickly or trickle for a while, but it never sloshes back uphill on its own.

4

Why It Must Stop, Not Just Slow Down

CLOSING THE ARGUMENT

Finitely many partitions + strictly decreasing objective = guaranteed halt

A monotonically decreasing sequence isn't automatically a terminating one — it could decrease forever by ever-smaller amounts. K-Means avoids that trap for a structural reason: there are only finitely many ways to partition n points into K clusters. That number is astronomically large, but it is finite.

①

Finite state space

Every reassignment step lands on one of a finite (if huge) number of possible partitions of the data.

②

Strict decrease per change

Whenever an iteration actually changes the assignment, the objective drops by a nonzero amount — no plateauing while still moving.

③

No repeats possible

Because the objective strictly decreases, the algorithm can never revisit the same partition twice.

Put those together: a process that can't repeat a state, drawing from a finite set of states, must run out of new states to visit. The algorithm is therefore forced to reach an iteration where no point wants to move — a fixed point — and it halts there.

Key insight

Convergence doesn't come from clever engineering — it's a direct consequence of pairing a strictly decreasing quantity with a finite space of possible states. That combination is what rules out infinite looping.

Local Minima and What That Means in Practice

THE CATCH

Guaranteed to stop — not guaranteed to stop at the best answer

The proof only guarantees that the objective stops decreasing. It says nothing about which fixed point the algorithm lands on. Different starting positions for the initial means can lead to entirely different final partitions — some far better than others.

local minimum
global minimum

Two different initializations of the cluster means can roll the algorithm into two different valleys. Both are places where K-Means stops — genuinely no point wants to move — but only one is the lowest possible objective value overall.

Real-life analogy

It's the same reason a hiker following "always walk downhill" can end up in a shallow valley instead of the true lowest point in the range — the rule guarantees you'll stop going downhill somewhere, but which valley you land in depends entirely on where you started walking.

CONSEQUENCE

Initialization becomes a real design decision

Because different starting means can land in different valleys, it's standard practice to run K-Means multiple times from different random starting points and keep the run with the lowest final objective — a direct, practical response to the local-minima problem this proof reveals.

IN PRACTICE

Slow theory, fast reality

The worst-case number of iterations before convergence can, in principle, be large — the finite-state argument only promises "eventually," not "quickly." In practice, on real datasets, the objective typically flattens out after a modest number of iterations, well short of any worst-case bound.

Watch out

"K-Means converged" is not the same claim as "K-Means found the best clustering." A single run reaching a stable fixed point can still be sitting in a mediocre local minimum — the fix is multiple restarts, not more iterations of the same run.

✓

Key Takeaways

1

The mean uniquely minimizes total squared distance to a fixed set of points — the geometric fact the entire proof leans on.

2

Both halves of a K-Means iteration — reassigning points and recomputing means — can only decrease the objective, never increase it.

3

Finitely many partitions plus a strictly decreasing objective means the algorithm cannot loop forever — it must reach a fixed point.

4

Convergence guarantees a local minimum, not the global one — which is exactly why initialization strategy and multiple restarts matter in practice.
