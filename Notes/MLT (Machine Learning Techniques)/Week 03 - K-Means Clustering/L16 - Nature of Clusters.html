Clustering Series · Lecture 16

Nature of K-Means Clusters —
Voronoi Regions & Their Limits

K-Means always converges — last lecture proved that. This lecture asks the next question: converge to what kind of shape? The answer turns out to be baked into the algorithm's arithmetic, and it explains both what K-Means is great at and where it quietly breaks.

§1 Condition §2 Linear Boundaries §3 Voronoi Regions §4 Limitations §5 Kernel Fix §6 Takeaways
01

What "Converged" Says About Each Point

At the fixed point, one simple statement is true of every single point: it must be at least as close to its own mean as to any other cluster's mean.

Equation (1)
Convergence Condition
$$\|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2 \;\leq\; \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2 \quad \forall j \neq i$$
True of every point $\mathbf{x}$ assigned to cluster $i$, for every other cluster $j$ — otherwise the reassignment step would have moved it
▶ Read the Condition in English
“A point $\mathbf{x}$ assigned to cluster $i$ is closer (in squared distance) to its own mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i$ than to any other cluster's mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_j$. If it weren't, the reassignment step would have switched it.”
▶ Symbol Legend
SymbolMeaningExample
$\mathbf{x}$ A data point assigned to cluster $i$ $\mathbf{x} = [1.5, 2.3]^T$
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_i$ Mean of the cluster containing $\mathbf{x}$ $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i = [1.2, 2.1]^T$
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_j$ Mean of some other cluster $j \neq i$ $\boldsymbol{\mu}_j = [4.0, 5.0]^T$
$\|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}\|^2$ Squared Euclidean distance $(x_1 - \mu_1)^2 + (x_2 - \mu_2)^2$
02

Why the Boundary Is a Straight Line

Squared Euclidean distance expands into a quadratic term, a linear term, and a constant. The quadratic term cancels out on both sides — leaving a purely linear condition.

Equation (2)
Expanding the Squared Distance Inequality
$$\|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2$$ $$\Downarrow$$ $$\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x} - 2\mathbf{x}^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_i + \boldsymbol{\mu}_i^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_i \;\leq\; \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x} - 2\mathbf{x}^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_j + \boldsymbol{\mu}_j^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_j$$ $$\Downarrow$$ $$2\mathbf{x}^T(\boldsymbol{\mu}_j - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i) \;\leq\; \boldsymbol{\mu}_j^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_j - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_i$$
The $\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}$ term cancels — what remains is linear in $\mathbf{x}$. The equality boundary is a hyperplane.
▶ Read the Derivation
Expanding $\|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}\|^2 = \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x} - 2\mathbf{x}^T\boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\mu}^T\boldsymbol{\mu}$ on both sides, the $\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}$ term appears identically and cancels. What remains is a linear inequality in $\mathbf{x}$: the decision boundary where the two sides are equal is a hyperplane — specifically, the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i$ and $\boldsymbol{\mu}_j$.
▶ The Perpendicular Bisector Between Two Means
Point $\mathbf{x}$ is assigned to whichever mean is nearer. As $\mathbf{x}$ slides across the red line, the two dashed distances become exactly equal — that line is the entire decision boundary and it is perfectly straight.
▶ Intuition
It's the same rule that decides which of two pizza shops delivers to your house first: not a curved zone, but a single straight line exactly halfway between the two shops. Everyone on your side of that line is closer to your shop; everyone past it belongs to the other.
03

Scaling Up: Voronoi Regions

The pairwise argument holds for every other cluster simultaneously. Each cluster is the intersection of $K-1$ half-spaces — a convex Voronoi region.

Equation (3)
Voronoi Region for Cluster $i$
$$V_i = \bigcap_{j \neq i} \left\{ \mathbf{x} : 2\mathbf{x}^T(\boldsymbol{\mu}_j - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i) \leq \boldsymbol{\mu}_j^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_j - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i^T\boldsymbol{\mu}_i \right\}$$
Each cluster region is the intersection of $K-1$ linear half-spaces — making it a convex polytope
▶ Read the Equation in English
“The Voronoi region $V_i$ for cluster $i$ is the set of points that satisfy the linear inequality against every other cluster $j$. The intersection of $K-1$ half-spaces is always a convex region.”
▶ Five Means, Five Convex Voronoi Regions
Every internal edge is a perpendicular bisector between two neighboring means. The space splits into exactly $K$ convex tiles — every straight edge, no curved boundary anywhere.
→
Key insight

K-Means doesn't just tend to produce convex clusters — it is structurally incapable of producing anything else. The intersection of half-spaces is convex by definition.

04

Where Convexity Becomes a Problem

Convex regions are exactly what you want when true clusters are blob-shaped. They are exactly the wrong tool for non-convex structure.

▶ Concentric Rings: True Clusters vs. K-Means
The dashed circles are the true rings. The red line is the best a 2-mean K-Means can do: a straight cut that slices through both rings, mixing inner and outer points on each side.
▶ Real-Life Analogy
It's like trying to separate the bullseye from the outer rings of a dartboard using a single straight fence. No matter where or at what angle you plant that fence, it will always cut across both rings — a straight boundary simply cannot isolate a ring-shaped region.
!
Structural Ceiling

✖ This isn't a bug that better initialization or more iterations can fix. It's a structural ceiling: as long as the algorithm assigns points using straight-line distance to a mean, ring-shaped, spiral, or otherwise non-convex clusters are out of reach.

05

The Fix: Kernel K-Means

The convexity limitation lives entirely in the original space $\mathbb{R}^d$. Map points into a higher-dimensional feature space where the same K-Means machinery can separate non-convex structure.

Equation (4)
Kernel K-Means Objective
$$\min_{\{z_i\}} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \|\phi(\mathbf{x}_i) - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_i}^{(\phi)}\|^2$$ $$\text{using only } K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \phi(\mathbf{x}_i)^T \phi(\mathbf{x}_j)$$
Same convex machinery, run in a feature space $\phi$ where the true clusters become linearly separable

Original Space

Concentric rings. No straight line can separate them. Standard K-Means is stuck.

Feature Space (via $\phi$)

The rings unfold into groups a hyperplane can separate. K-Means's convex regions become the right tool again.

Kernel Trick

We never compute $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ directly — only $K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$. Same reassign-and-recompute loop, but in $\phi$-space.

▶ Real-Life Analogy
Think of trying to separate the colors of a swirled marble cake with a single straight knife cut — impossible in the pan. Unroll each swirl into its own straight layer first, and one clean cut suddenly separates everything. The kernel map is that unrolling step.
→
Connecting to spectral clustering

Kernelized K-Means is closely related to spectral clustering, which partitions data using the eigenvectors of a kernel (or similarity) matrix rather than iterating means directly — a different route to the same non-convexity fix.

06

Key Takeaways

Linear Boundary

The convergence condition $\|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_i\|^2 \leq \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2$, once expanded, is linear in $\mathbf{x}$ — only a hyperplane survives.

Voronoi Regions

That hyperplane is the perpendicular bisector between two means; intersecting $K-1$ of them per cluster produces convex Voronoi regions.

Non-Convex Failures

K-Means can only recover convex structure — never rings, spirals, or other non-convex shapes. Kernel K-Means fixes this.
