Nature of K-Means Clusters —
Voronoi Regions &
Their Limits

K-Means always converges — last lecture proved that. This lecture asks the next question: converge to what kind of shape? The answer turns out to be baked into the algorithm's arithmetic, and it explains both what K-Means is great at and where it quietly breaks.

Builds on · Lloyd's algorithm, the convergence proof Core idea · The convergence condition is a linear inequality in disguise Output · Every cluster is convex — which is a feature and a limitation
1

What "Converged" Actually Says About Each Point

STARTING CONDITION

Every point is closer to its own mean than to anyone else's

At the fixed point the previous lecture proved K-Means must reach, one simple statement is true of every single point in the dataset: for a point x assigned to cluster i with mean μᵢ, it must be at least as close to μᵢ as to any other cluster's mean μⱼ — otherwise the reassignment step would have moved it.

‖x − μi‖²  ≤  ‖x − μj‖²    for every other cluster j true of every point, for every pair of clusters, at convergence — this single inequality is about to explain the entire shape of a K-Means cluster

It looks like a throwaway fact — almost the definition of "assigned to the nearest mean." But expanding it algebraically reveals something much stronger about the geometry that inequality implies.

2

Why the Boundary Between Two Clusters Is a Straight Line

THE ALGEBRA

Expand the squared distances — the curvature cancels out

Squared Euclidean distance expands into a quadratic term, a linear term, and a constant. Watch what happens to the quadratic term when both sides of the inequality are expanded:

‖x − μi‖²  ≤  ‖x − μj‖² xTx − 2xTμi + μiTμi  ≤  xTx − 2xTμj + μjTμj — the xTx term appears on both sides and cancels — 2xT(μj − μi)  ≤  μjTμj − μiTμi what remains is linear in x — no x² term survives. The boundary where equality holds is therefore a hyperplane: specifically, the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining μᵢ and μⱼ.
The bisector boundary between two means
closer to μᵢ closer to μⱼ bisector (the boundary)

Point x is assigned to whichever mean is nearer. As x slides across the red line, the two dashed distances become exactly equal — that line is the entire decision boundary between these two clusters, and it is perfectly straight.

Real-life analogy

It's the same rule that decides which of two pizza shops delivers to your house first: not a curved zone, but a single straight line exactly halfway between the two shops. Everyone on your side of that line is closer to your shop; everyone past it belongs to the other.

3

Scaling Up: Voronoi Regions

FROM ONE PAIR TO ALL K CLUSTERS

Each cluster is the intersection of K−1 half-spaces

The pairwise argument above holds for every other cluster simultaneously. A point in cluster i must sit on the "μᵢ side" of the bisector against cluster 1, and against cluster 2, and so on for all K−1 other clusters at once. Intersecting K−1 half-spaces produces a single convex region — this is precisely the definition of a Voronoi region.

Five means, five convex Voronoi regions, zero gaps

Every internal edge here is itself a perpendicular bisector between two neighboring means. However many clusters K there are, the space splits into exactly K convex tiles — every straight edge, no curved boundary anywhere.

Key insight

K-Means doesn't just tend to produce convex clusters — it is structurally incapable of producing anything else. The intersection of half-spaces is convex by definition, full stop.

4

Where Convexity Becomes a Problem

THE LIMITATION

Only linearly separable structure is recoverable

Convex regions are exactly what you want when true clusters really are blob-shaped and roughly separable by straight lines. They are exactly the wrong tool the moment true structure is non-convex — and nested rings are the cleanest example of that failure.

Concentric rings: the true clusters vs. what K-Means can draw

The dashed circles are the true rings we want as clusters. The red line is the best a 2-mean K-Means can do: a straight cut that slices through both rings, mixing inner and outer points on each side instead of separating them.

Real-life analogy

It's like trying to separate the bullseye from the outer rings of a dartboard using a single straight fence. No matter where or at what angle you plant that fence, it will always cut across both rings — a straight boundary simply cannot isolate a ring-shaped region.

Watch out

This isn't a bug that better initialization or more iterations can fix. It's a structural ceiling: as long as the algorithm assigns points using straight-line distance to a mean, ring-shaped, spiral, or otherwise non-convex clusters are out of reach.

5

The Fix: Move the Straight Lines Somewhere They Work

KERNEL K-MEANS

Same convex machinery — a different, better-behaved space

The convexity limitation lives entirely in the original space ℝd. Exactly as with kernel PCA, the fix is to map points into a higher-dimensional feature space via φ, where the same straight-line, convex K-Means machinery — run using only kernel evaluations K(Xᵢ, Xⱼ) = φ(Xᵢ)ᵀφ(Xⱼ) — can now separate structure that was non-convex back in the original space.

→

Original space

Concentric rings. No straight line can separate them. Standard K-Means is stuck.

→

Feature space (via φ)

The rings unfold into groups a hyperplane can separate. K-Means's convex regions become the right tool again.

Worth noting

Kernelized K-Means is closely related to spectral clustering, which partitions data using the eigenvectors of a kernel (or similarity) matrix rather than iterating means directly — two different routes to the same non-convexity fix.

Real-life analogy

Think of trying to separate the colors of a swirled marble cake with a single straight knife cut — impossible in the pan. Unroll each swirl into its own straight layer first, and one clean cut suddenly separates everything. The kernel map is that unrolling step.

Key insight

Nothing about the K-Means algorithm changes under kernelization — reassign, then recompute — only the space it operates in changes, via the kernel trick. Convexity is still required; the trick is finding a space where the true clusters happen to satisfy it.

Key Takeaways

1

The convergence condition ‖x−μᵢ‖² ≤ ‖x−μⱼ‖², once expanded, is linear in x — the quadratic terms cancel and only a hyperplane condition survives.

2

That hyperplane is the perpendicular bisector between two means; intersecting K−1 of them per cluster produces convex Voronoi regions.

3

Because every cluster is an intersection of half-spaces, K-Means can only recover linearly separable, convex structure — never rings, spirals, or other non-convex shapes.

4

Kernel K-Means resolves this the same way kernel PCA does: keep the convex machinery, just run it in a feature space where the true clusters become convex.
