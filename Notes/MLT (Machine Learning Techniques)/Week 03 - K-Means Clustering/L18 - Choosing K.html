Choosing K —
Balancing Fit
Against Complexity

Every earlier lecture quietly assumed K was handed to us. It never is. This lecture confronts the last open question in K-Means head-on: left unchecked, the objective function wants K as large as possible — so something else has to want the opposite.

1

The Problem with Minimizing the Objective Alone

THE TRAP

Unconstrained, the objective is happiest at K = n

Recall the K-Means objective — total squared distance from every point to its cluster's mean:

f(z1, …, zn)  =  Σi=1n ‖xi − μzi‖² the standard objective — total squared distance from every point to its assigned mean

If K is free to grow, the minimizer is trivial: set K = n, give every point its own cluster, and the mean of a single-point cluster is that point itself. Every distance term becomes exactly zero.

Watching the objective collapse to zero as K grows
K = 2

Real compression

Two means summarize ten points. High, but meaningful, objective.

K = 5

Tighter, but shrinking fast

Each mean now covers just 2 points — the objective has visibly dropped.

K = n

Zero objective, zero use

Every point is its own mean. Distance is zero everywhere — and nothing has been learned.

Each ring is a cluster mean. By K = n, every ring sits exactly on its one point — perfect "fit," but a completely useless model: no grouping, no compression, no insight into structure.

Real-life analogy

It's like summarizing a book by quoting every single sentence verbatim — technically a perfect, zero-loss "summary," but it has compressed nothing and taught you nothing you didn't already have. A useful summary has to lose some detail to gain any value.

Watch out

Never select K by comparing raw objective values across different K. The objective is monotonically non-increasing in K by construction — it will always vote for more clusters, right up to the useless extreme of K = n.

2

Fixing It: Add a Penalty That Wants the Opposite

THE FIX

Make complexity cost something

If the objective always prefers larger K, the selection criterion needs a second term that always prefers smaller K. Adding the two together produces a total cost with a genuine interior minimum:

Total Cost(K)  =  Objective(K)  +  Penalty(K) Objective(K) decreases (weakly) in K; Penalty(K) increases in K — their sum can have a genuine interior minimum
Two opposing trends meet at K*
Objective(K) — falls as K grows Penalty(K) — rises as K grows Total cost — dips, then rises

K* sits where the total-cost curve bottoms out — not where either individual curve is smallest. Past that point, added complexity costs more in penalty than it saves in fit.

Real-life analogy

It's the same logic behind deciding how many suitcases to pack for a trip: more suitcases let you bring more things (better "fit" to everything you might want), but each extra suitcase costs you real hassle at the airport. The right number balances both, rather than maximizing either alone.

3

Two Concrete Penalties: AIC and BIC

TURNING THE IDEA INTO A FORMULA

Both criteria score a model by likelihood minus a complexity charge

AIC and BIC both formalize "Total Cost" using a probabilistic lens: assume the data was generated by some model with parameters (the cluster means, in this case), score how well those parameters explain the data via their likelihood, and subtract a penalty that grows with the number of parameters — which, in K-Means, grows with K.

AIC

Akaike Information Criterion

Penalizes complexity proportionally to K itself. Comparatively lenient — tends to favor larger K than BIC.

BIC

Bayesian Information Criterion

Penalizes complexity proportionally to K·log(n). Grows harsher as the dataset gets larger — noticeably more stringent than AIC.

AIC  ∝  −log-likelihood + c₁ · K      BIC  ∝  −log-likelihood + c₂ · K log(n) same shape, different penalty growth rate — BIC's K·log(n) term outpaces AIC's plain K term as n grows, so BIC systematically prefers smaller K on larger datasets
CriterionPenalty termComplexity stanceGrows with n?

Worth noting

Both criteria lean on "likelihood," which presumes some probabilistic model of how the data was generated (commonly, that points scatter around their true cluster mean according to a Gaussian). The penalty's job either way is the same: make additional clusters earn their keep, rather than being free.

The Full Story: Four Lectures on K-Means

SERIES RECAP

From "does it converge" to "how many clusters"

This closes the K-Means arc. Four questions, asked in order, now each have a real answer:

15lecture

Convergence

Lloyd's algorithm is guaranteed to converge — the objective decreases monotonically and the state space is finite — but only to a local optimum, not necessarily the global one.

16lecture

Cluster nature

Clusters produced by Lloyd's algorithm are Voronoi regions — convex intersections of half-spaces — which is exactly why non-convex structure like concentric rings defeats it.

17lecture

Initialization

Where you start decides which local optimum you reach. K-Means++ seeds centroids by sampling proportional to distance from what's already chosen, trading extra computation for a real quality guarantee.

22lecture

Choice of K

The objective alone always prefers more clusters. Balancing it against a complexity penalty — as AIC and BIC both do — is what turns K into a well-posed choice rather than a trivial one.

The thread connecting all four

Every one of these results traces back to the same handful of ideas: squared Euclidean distance, means as optimal centers, and straight-line (half-space) decision boundaries. Understanding those fundamentals is what makes convergence, cluster shape, initialization, and model selection all fall out as consequences rather than separate rules to memorize.

✓

Key Takeaways

1

Minimizing the raw K-Means objective alone always drives K toward n, where every point is its own (useless) cluster.

2

A workable selection rule needs a penalty term that increases with K, so the sum Objective(K) + Penalty(K) has a genuine interior minimum K*.

3

AIC penalizes complexity proportional to K; BIC penalizes proportional to K·log(n) and is correspondingly more conservative, especially on larger datasets.

4

Together, Lectures 15–22 answer convergence, cluster shape, initialization, and K — the four practical questions any real use of K-Means has to face.
