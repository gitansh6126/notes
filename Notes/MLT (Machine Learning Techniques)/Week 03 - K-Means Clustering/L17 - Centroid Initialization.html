Initializing K-Means —
From Random Guesses

Lloyd's algorithm converges no matter where the centroids start. But "converges" and "converges somewhere good" are different promises — this lecture is entirely about closing that gap, from the cheapest possible starting strategy to a genuinely principled one.

Why Initialization Is Still an Open Problem

Convergence was never in question. Quality is. This lecture works through three initialization strategies in order of increasing sophistication.

Equation (1)
The Local Minima Problem
$$\mathcal{J}(Z, \boldsymbol{\mu}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_i}\|^2$$
Lloyd's algorithm finds a local minimum of $\mathcal{J}$. Which local minimum depends entirely on where the centroids start.

02

Naive Initialization: Random Assignment

The simplest possible rule: give every point a uniformly random label from $1$ to $K$, then compute each label's mean as the starting centroid.

Equation (2)
Random Label Initialization
$$z_i \sim \text{Uniform}\{1, \dots, K\} \quad \text{then} \quad \boldsymbol{\mu}_k^{(0)} = \frac{1}{n_k}\sum_{i:z_i=k} \mathbf{x}_i$$
Labels drawn uniformly at random, then centroids are the means of those random groups
▶ Read the Equation in English
“Each point $\mathbf{x}_i$ gets a random label $z_i$ from $1$ to $K$, chosen uniformly. Then for each label $k$, we average all points that happened to get that label — that average becomes the starting centroid $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k^{(0)}$.”
▶ Intuition
It's like assigning meeting spots by handing out ticket numbers at random rather than looking at where people actually live — the "center" you compute from a random subset ends up somewhere near the middle of the whole city, useless as a starting guess for anyone's actual neighborhood. All $K$ means tend to land near the global center of mass, which is a terrible place to start.
!
Watch out

Because it ignores data distribution entirely, this strategy is especially prone to starting all $K$ means bunched near the global center of mass — a textbook setup for getting stuck in a poor local minimum.

03

Random Centroid Selection

Instead of averaging a random subset, use $K$ real data points, drawn uniformly at random, directly as the starting means.

Equation (3)
Random Centroid Selection
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_1^{(0)}, \dots, \boldsymbol{\mu}_K^{(0)} \sim \text{Uniform}\{\mathbf{x}_1, \dots, \mathbf{x}_n\} \text{ without replacement}$$
Pick $K$ data points uniformly at random — they become the initial centroids
▶ Read the Equation in English
“Choose $K$ distinct data points uniformly at random from the dataset. These are the starting means. Every other point is then assigned to its nearest of these $K$ points.”
▶ Comparison: Random Assignment vs. Random Selection
PropertyRandom assignmentRandom centroid selection
Centroids are actual data points? No Yes
Centroids inside data density? No — may fall in empty space Yes — guaranteed on a real point
Can $K$ centroids cluster together? Yes — all near global center Yes — if unlucky draws are in same region
▶ Intuition
This guarantees the starting centroids actually sit inside the data — a real improvement over averaging noise. But it is still pure luck whether the $K$ draws land in different regions or accidentally cluster together. The standard workaround: run Lloyd's algorithm multiple times from independent random draws and keep whichever run reaches the lowest final objective.
04

K-Means++: Spreading the Seeds on Purpose

If two starting centroids are near each other, they're competing for the same region while some other region gets no representative. K-Means++ builds spread-out-ness directly into the selection rule.

Equation (4)
K-Means++ Sampling Score
$$\text{score}(\mathbf{x}) = \min_{j=1,\dots,l-1} \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2$$
A point's score is its squared distance to the nearest already-chosen centroid — far-from-everything points score highest
▶ Read the Equation in English
“For each remaining (unchosen) data point $\mathbf{x}$, compute its squared Euclidean distance to each already-chosen centroid $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1, \dots, \boldsymbol{\mu}_{l-1}$. Keep the minimum of those distances as its score. Then sample the next centroid with probability proportional to this score.”
▶ K-Means++ Algorithm Steps
StepActionScoring rule
1 Choose $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1$ uniformly at random from the dataset No score needed — uniform
2 For $l = 2, \dots, K$, compute score for each unchosen point $\displaystyle \min_{j
3 Sample $\boldsymbol{\mu}_l$ with probability $\propto$ score $P(\mathbf{x}) \propto \text{score}(\mathbf{x})$
▶ Watching K-Means++ Seeds Spread Out
Darker, larger dots have higher scores. By step 3, all three natural blobs have a representative.
▶ Why Probabilistic, Not Deterministic
A deterministic "always pick the $\max$ score" rule is exploitable: a single adversarially placed outlier would get chosen as a centroid every single time, wasting a centroid on noise. Sampling proportional to score still favors far-away points strongly, but gives every point a nonzero chance — which is precisely what allows K-Means++ to carry a provable guarantee on the expected quality of its final clustering relative to the true optimum.

05

Summary Comparison

Speed vs. guarantees: each strategy strikes a different tradeoff.

▶ Strategy Comparison Table
StrategyComputation per initData-aware?Quality guarantee
Random assignment $O(n)$ No None
Random centroid selection $O(nK)$ Partially None (needs multiple restarts)
K-Means++ $O(nK)$ Yes, explicitly Expected $O(\log K)$-competitive with optimal
▶ Practical Guidance
Uniform random initialization remains common because it is cheap and simple, especially at very high dimensionality where computing scores repeatedly adds overhead. K-Means++ costs more per initialization but earns a real theoretical guarantee in exchange — and in practice tends to need far fewer restarts to reach a good clustering.
→
Standard practice

Run K-Means++ once (or a few times), not hundreds of random restarts. The $O(\log K)$ competitive ratio means K-Means++ provably finds a clustering within a logarithmic factor of the optimum — a guarantee neither of the random strategies can offer.

06

Key Takeaways

Random Labels Fail

Random-label initialization ignores data structure entirely, producing means bunched near the global center.

Random Points Better

Random centroid selection guarantees centroids sit on real data, but still needs multiple restarts.

K-Means++ Wins

K-Means++ builds spread-out seeding into the sampling rule via $\text{score}(\mathbf{x}) = \min \|\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|^2$, with an $O(\log K)$ guarantee.
