— the difference between wanting something and actually being a customer for it —

Continuing from Part 1 Same needs/wants/desires ladder — now we add the one ingredient that turns a desire into an actual purchase.

What Is Demand?

Demand = Desire + Ability to Pay + Willingness to Pay

Miss even one ingredient, and it's not demand — just a wish.

Desire

You actually want the concert ticket.

Ability to Pay

You genuinely have enough money in the bank to buy it.

Willingness to Pay

You choose to spend that money on the ticket instead of saving it for rent.

Real Life: Two Friends, One Concert

Friend A has enough savings for a ticket but decides to save it for rent instead — desire and ability exist, but not willingness. Friend B has the same savings and buys the ticket without hesitation.

Analogy

Only Friend B represents real demand — Friend A's want, no matter how genuine, never becomes a purchase.

Desire vs. Demand

Desire = Dream

A student wanting a Ferrari — a genuine, emotional want with no money behind it. It may never happen.

Demand = Dream + Money

A businessman wanting a Ferrari and actually driving one home — the exact same want, backed by real purchasing power.

Purchasing Power

Purchasing Power

Real Life

A video game's in-game shop — your character can admire the legendary sword all day, but without enough in-game gold, it stays locked behind glass.

Analogy

Wanting the sword (desire) never converts into owning it without the gold (purchasing power) to unlock the purchase.

Microeconomics, Revisited

The IPL Auction Example

Real Life

Every IPL franchise wants the best batsman, bowler, captain, and wicketkeeper — but the salary cap is fixed, so they have to prioritize.

Analogy

A fixed budget forcing a single team to choose between star players is microeconomics in miniature — one unit, one constrained decision.

Macroeconomics: Tariffs on a Whole Economy

Real Life

A new tariff doesn't just affect one importer — it ripples through GDP, employment, and inflation nationwide.

Analogy

Same event, but viewed from the satellite-photo level instead of the single-ant level — that shift in altitude is exactly what separates macro from micro.

Households ⇄ Firms

A farmers' market: farmers (households) sell their labor and produce, get paid, then spend that same money buying tools and goods from the town's shops (firms) — round and round it goes.

Three Types of Scarcity

Demand-Induced

What changes: demand rises, supply stays the same.

A local restaurant goes viral overnight — same kitchen, same seating, suddenly triple the customers and a two-hour wait.

Supply-Induced

What changes: supply falls, demand stays about the same.

A storm wipes out a season's vegetable harvest — shoppers want the same amount of produce, but the shelves are simply empty.

Structural

What changes: access is uneven, not the total amount.

A city block with five pharmacies within walking distance, versus a village where the nearest clinic is three hours away — same country, wildly different access.

A want only becomes demand once money and willingness show up behind it — and scarcity isn't always about running out; sometimes it's about who gets to reach the shelf at all.
