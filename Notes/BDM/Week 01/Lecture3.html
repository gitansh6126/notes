Utility: Satisfaction, Not Usefulness

Utility Is Subjective

Real Life

The exact same movie — one friend calls it the best film of the year, another falls asleep halfway through.

Analogy

Same product (the jacket, the movie, the meal), completely different satisfaction — utility lives in the consumer, not the object itself.

Marginality: One Decision at a Time

Step-by-Step Decisions

Real Life

Filling up a car's gas tank a little at a time whenever you pass a station, rather than signing up for a lifetime supply of fuel in one go.

Analogy

Each chocolate, each glass of water, each extra unit is its own separate decision — that one-at-a-time habit is what "marginal" thinking actually means.

Marginal Utility

Charging a near-dead phone: the jump from 0% to 20% feels like relief. The jump from 80% to 100% barely registers — same 20 percentage points, wildly different satisfaction.

MU = Extra Satisfaction, One More Unit

Real Life

That relief jump from 0% to 20% battery is huge marginal utility; the jump from 80% to 100% is nearly unnoticed marginal utility.

Analogy

Total charge (Total Utility) still goes up the whole time — but each additional percent (Marginal Utility) matters less and less.

Total Utility vs. Marginal Utility

Filling a Bathtub

Real Life

Total Utility is the water level in the tub. Marginal Utility is how fast the tap is currently flowing — the level (TU) keeps rising even as the flow rate (MU) slows down.

Analogy

The tub stops filling — TU peaks — the exact moment the flow rate hits zero (MU = 0). Turn the tap the wrong way (negative MU), and the water level actually starts dropping.

Negative Utility

Real Life

Rewatching the same episode of a show for the tenth time in a row — what used to be entertaining now just feels tedious.

Analogy

At that point, one more unit doesn't just add nothing — it actively makes you worse off, exactly like that 6th ice cream making you feel sick.

Assumptions of LDMU — ICRTTU

I
Identical Goods

A taste test comparing apple to apple — not apple to orange. Switching flavors partway breaks the pattern.

C
Continuous Consumption

A marathon runner drinking water at every mile marker — not one sip today and another next month.

R
Rational Consumer

A careful shopper doing the mental math — not an impulse buy driven purely by emotion.

T
Taste Constant

Loving the same genre of music throughout the whole listening session — not flipping preferences halfway through.

T
Utility Measurable

Assuming there's a working "happiness scale" in the background, even though satisfaction is really a feeling, not a number.

U
Uniform Unit Size

Comparing one normal slice to the next normal slice — not one regular slice against one giant novelty slice.

Business Pricing: Why Bulk Is Cheaper

The Warehouse Club Strategy

Real Life

A wholesale club charging full price for your first bulk item, but discounting each additional one — the first roll of paper towels feels essential, the twelfth much less exciting.

Analogy

Since your marginal utility for extra units keeps falling, the store has to lower the price to keep tempting you — exactly the backpack pricing pattern (₹300 → ₹550 → ₹750 total, cheaper per bag each time).

Total satisfaction keeps climbing right up until the extra unit stops adding anything — and one bite past that point is where satisfaction quietly turns into regret.
