Unlimited Wants > Limited Resources = Scarcity → Need for Choice → Economics

Every topic this term is really just this one sentence, examined from a different angle.

Real Life

A buffet table with a mountain of food, but your plate is only so big — you physically cannot pile on everything you see.

Analogy

Your plate's limited size is scarcity; deciding what goes on it is the "choice" half of the equation — that's economics in one bite.

Needs vs. Wants vs. Desires

Same idea, three levels of urgency and affordability.

Need

Real life: water and a headlamp on a camping trip — without them, the trip becomes dangerous.

Need = Live

Want

Real life: a more comfortable sleeping bag — nice to have, but the trip still works without it.

Want = Enjoy

Desire

Real life: wanting the premium four-season tent — and already having the cash in your wallet to buy it right now.

Desire = Afford

Infinite (Insatiable) Wants

Real Life

A video game's endless upgrade tree — the moment you finally afford the better sword, the game immediately shows you an even better one just out of reach.

Analogy

Satisfying one want (books → laptop → bike → car → house) doesn't end the cycle — it just reveals the next tier.

Not the Same as Selfishness

A parent's "want" for their child's better education is still an infinite want — it's just aimed at someone else.

Analogy

"Insatiable" describes the never-ending pattern, not the target — wanting good things for others fits the same cycle.

Goods, Services & Resources

Goods vs. Services

Real Life

A loaf of bread on a shelf (good) vs. a barber cutting your hair (service) — one is a thing, the other is an action performed for you.

Resources & Scarcity

Real Life

A kitchen's pantry — flour, butter, and sugar all run out eventually, no matter how many cake orders keep coming in.

Analogy

Land, oil, and minerals are the pantry ingredients of the whole economy — finite stock, unlimited orders.

Economic Goods vs. Free Goods

Free Good

Real life: sunlight streaming through a window — nobody had to grow it, mine it, or ship it to you.

No scarcity, no cost to produce, available to everyone naturally.

Economic Good

Real life: the tomatoes grown in a greenhouse — land, water, labor, and glass panels all had to be paid for.

Scarce, requires resources, has a real cost even if the price tag says otherwise.

Exam Trick: "Free" hospital treatment is still an Economic Good — the patient pays ₹0, but doctors, buildings, and medicine still consumed scarce resources. Free of cost ≠ Free good.

Factors of Production (LLCE)

Every business, in the language of a bakery.

🌍

Land

Natural resources from the earth.

The kitchen space and raw flour/sugar sourced from nature.

🙌

Labour

Human effort in production.

The baker's hands, actually kneading and shaping the dough.

🏭

Capital

Productive assets, not money.

The oven and mixer — tools that help make more goods.

💡

Enterprise

The organizer who combines the other three.

The person who decides to open the bakery and puts it all together.

Production

Any step that moves something closer to satisfying a human want counts as production — not just factory assembly lines.

The Economic Problem

Constrained Choice

Real Life

A household with one paycheck and a long wishlist — rent, groceries, a vacation, a new phone. The paycheck doesn't stretch to cover all of it this month.

Analogy

A government budget works the same way — ₹100 crore can't fully fund both education and roads, so it has to be split by priority.

Microeconomics vs. Macroeconomics

Microeconomics

Studies individual units — one consumer, one business, one worker.

Real life: looking through a magnifying glass at a single ant — how does it react to one obstacle on its own path?

Macroeconomics

Studies the whole economy — GDP, inflation, national employment.

Real life: a satellite photo of the entire anthill — how does the whole colony's traffic pattern shift?

Constrained Optimization

Making the Best of a Fixed Budget

Real Life

Grocery shopping with exactly ₹1000 in your wallet — you can't buy everything on the shelf, so you fill the cart with the combination that makes you happiest at checkout.

Analogy

Every economic decision is that shopping cart — a fixed constraint, and a search for the combination that gets the most out of it.

Every definition in this unit is one sentence in disguise: wants never stop, resources never stretch far enough, and economics is just the study of choosing well anyway.
